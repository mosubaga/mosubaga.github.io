The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

The case that changed detective fiction forever.

Synopsis

The Story

Roger Ackroyd is a wealthy widower living in the peaceful English village of King's Abbot. Shortly after confiding to his friend Dr. Sheppard that he has received a letter revealing the identity of his late fiancée's murderer, Ackroyd is found dead in his locked study, stabbed through the neck with an ornamental dagger.

Hercule Poirot, who has retired to the village to grow vegetable marrows, is persuaded to investigate. Assisted by Dr. Sheppard — who narrates the tale — Poirot methodically examines the evidence, the alibis, and the peculiarities of each suspect's behaviour. As he peels back layer after layer of deception, he uncovers not merely a killer, but a secret that will send shockwaves through the entire village.

No further details are given here — the ending of this novel must be experienced firsthand.

Dramatis Personae

The Characters

A

Roger Ackroyd

The Victim

A wealthy, genial widower and pillar of King's Abbot society. Ackroyd is a man of settled habits, regular hours, and unsuspected secrets. His murder sets the entire investigation in motion.

S

Dr. James Sheppard

Narrator

The village doctor and chronicler of events. Sheppard is intelligent, composed, and deeply embedded in the social fabric of King's Abbot. He narrates events with a precision that will later seem, on reflection, rather remarkable.

F

Flora Ackroyd

The Niece

Roger's spirited niece and heir. Flora is engaged to Ralph Paton, and her fierce loyalty to him — even as evidence mounts against him — makes her a sympathetic and significant figure.

R

Ralph Paton

The Stepson

Ackroyd's stepson and the primary suspect following the murder. Ralph is charming and irresponsible, with a secret marriage and pressing debts. His unexplained disappearance makes him look very guilty indeed.

M

Mrs. Cecil Ackroyd

Sister-in-Law

Roger's widowed sister-in-law, a fretful, gossipy woman who has been living on Roger's charity. Her need for money gives her a motive, and her nervous disposition makes her an unreliable witness.

G

Geoffrey Raymond

Secretary

Ackroyd's efficient young secretary, present on the fatal evening. Raymond is competent and professional, though his proximity to events and knowledge of Ackroyd's affairs place him under scrutiny.

Trivia
Behind the Book

Trivia & Notes

  • 01

    Published in 1926, The Murder of Roger Ackroyd is widely considered one of the most controversial and celebrated detective novels ever written, largely because of its audacious narrative device.

  • 02

    The book provoked furious debate among readers and critics upon publication, with some arguing it violated the rules of fair play in detective fiction. Christie maintained the clues were all present in the text.

  • 03

    The Detection Club, founded in 1930, subsequently debated whether certain narrative techniques should be formally prohibited in detective fiction — a direct consequence of this novel.

  • 04

    T.S. Eliot was among the novel's champions, writing admiringly of its construction. It has never been out of print since its first publication.

  • 05

    Christie later said the book's genesis came from a suggestion by her brother-in-law, Lord Louis Mountbatten, who proposed the surprising narrative angle as a clever challenge to the genre.

  • 06

    The story is set in the fictional village of King's Abbot, which Christie based on the English countryside she knew from her own life in Devon.
