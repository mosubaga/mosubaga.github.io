The A.B.C. Murders

A serial killer taunts Poirot with alphabetical victims.

Synopsis

The Story

Hercule Poirot receives a mocking letter from an anonymous correspondent signing himself “A.B.C.” — warning that a murder will be committed in the town of Andover on a given date. Despite Poirot's efforts to alert the authorities, a woman named Alice Ascher is found murdered in her small tobacco shop. By the body lies an open copy of the A.B.C. Railway Guide.

Further letters follow, each promising another murder in alphabetical order: Bexhill, Churston. Each murder follows the same pattern — a victim whose name matches the location in the alphabet, and that damning railway guide left open at the relevant entry. As the death toll rises, panic spreads across England, and even Scotland Yard seems helpless against this methodical killer.

Yet Poirot refuses to accept the obvious. Something about the pattern troubles him, something that suggests the murders are not quite what they seem. The A.B.C. Murders is a masterclass in misdirection — both of the detective and the reader.

Dramatis Personae

The Characters

H

Captain Hastings

Narrator

Reunited with Poirot after years in Argentina, Hastings once again serves as the chronicler of events. His presence allows Christie to use the familiar Watson-Holmes dynamic while subverting the reader's expectations at every turn.

C

Alexander Bonaparte Cust

The Suspect

A travelling stocking salesman with the initials A.B.C. who has suffered from severe headaches and blackouts since the Great War. Cust finds himself near the scene of each murder and is terrified by what the evidence seems to suggest about himself.

C

Inspector Crome

Scotland Yard

The ambitious young detective assigned by Scotland Yard to the A.B.C. case. Crome is dismissive of Poirot's methods and confident in his own conventional approach — a confidence that will prove ill-founded.

F

Franklin Clarke

Country Gentleman

The energetic, attractive brother of the third victim, Sir Carmichael Clarke. Franklin takes an active role in the investigation, organising a group of victims' relatives to assist Poirot. His energy and resourcefulness make him both useful and suspicious.

M

Megan Barnard

Sister of Victim

The sharp-tongued, direct sister of Betty Barnard, the second victim. Megan is grieving but not passive — her clear-sighted analysis of events and her refusal to sentimentalise make her one of the most credible witnesses.

T

Thora Grey

Secretary

The efficient secretary to the third victim, Sir Carmichael Clarke. Thora is composed, attractive, and in possession of information she does not immediately volunteer — which naturally draws Poirot's attention.

Trivia
Behind the Book

Trivia & Notes

  • 01

    The A.B.C. Murders was one of the first Christie novels to employ the technique of the unreliable red herring at the level of plot structure, rather than individual clues.

  • 02

    Christie introduces the concept of a "serial killer" narrative years before criminology popularised the term — the novel's structure anticipates the modern procedural thriller.

  • 03

    The novel features a unique narrative experiment: alternating chapters from Hastings's first-person account and a close third-person perspective on the mysterious A.B.C. suspect.

  • 04

    The A.B.C. Railway Guide was a real publication — an actual British timetable book issued alphabetically by station name — giving Christie's plot a pleasingly practical basis in reality.

  • 05

    Christie uses the novel to examine collective psychology and mass hysteria, showing how a pattern can make otherwise rational people — including detectives — leap to entirely wrong conclusions.

  • 06

    Poirot's key insight in this novel demonstrates his core principle: to question the obvious when the obvious is exactly what someone wants you to see.
