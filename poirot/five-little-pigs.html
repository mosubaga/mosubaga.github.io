Five Little Pigs

Can a sixteen-year-old murder be solved from memory alone?

Synopsis

The Story

A young woman named Carla Lemarchant approaches Hercule Poirot with an unusual commission: she wants him to investigate a murder that was committed sixteen years ago, for which her mother, Caroline Crale, was tried and convicted. Caroline died in prison a year after her conviction, but Carla has always believed in her mother's innocence — and she has a letter to prove it.

The victim was Amyas Crale, a celebrated and temperamental painter. He was poisoned with coniine while working on a portrait of his mistress at the family's Cornish home. Five people were present that day — the “five little pigs” of the title — each with their own account of what they saw, what they felt, and what they concealed.

Poirot proceeds with extraordinary delicacy: there are no physical clues, no crime scene to examine. He must work entirely from human memory, interviewing each witness and reading their written accounts. Five Little Pigs is considered Christie's own favourite among the Poirot novels — and it shows.

Dramatis Personae

The Characters

C

Carla Lemarchant

The Client

Née Caroline Crale, Carla was barely five years old when her father was murdered and her mother convicted. Now a young woman about to be married, she is driven by love, doubt, and a fierce desire for the truth.

C

Caroline Crale

The Convicted

Amyas Crale's wife, convicted of his murder. A woman of great loyalty and quiet strength, she was known to have quarrelled violently with her husband over his latest affair. Whether her conviction was just is the central question of the novel.

A

Amyas Crale

The Victim

A brilliant, selfish, and charismatic painter. Amyas Crale was a man of enormous talent and deplorable personal conduct — unfaithful, careless of others' feelings, but entirely devoted to his art. His death robbed the world of a great painter.

E

Elsa Greer

The Mistress

The young, beautiful woman who was sitting for her portrait on the day of the murder. Elsa was Amyas's current obsession and the subject of the fatal quarrel. Sixteen years on, she is Lady Dittisham — wealthy, brittle, and still haunted.

B

Philip Blake

The Friend

Amyas Crale's oldest friend, a city businessman who was staying at the house on the fateful day. Philip is a practical man with deeply conventional views, whose account of events is coloured by his long-standing dislike of Caroline.

W

Cecilia Williams

The Governess

The Crales' governess, a woman of rigorous principle and devoted to Caroline's daughter Carla. Miss Williams's precise, honest account of events is among the most valuable Poirot receives — and the most unexpected in its implications.

Trivia
Behind the Book

Trivia & Notes

  • 01

    Agatha Christie cited Five Little Pigs as her personal favourite among the Poirot novels, praising its unusual structure and the depth of character it allowed her to develop.

  • 02

    The novel's entire investigation is conducted without a crime scene — every clue comes from the memory and psychology of the five witnesses. It is a detective novel almost entirely of the mind.

  • 03

    Christie structures the novel in three acts: Poirot's initial interviews, the five written accounts (one per witness), and Poirot's final synthesis — a triptych that reveals how the same events look radically different depending on who is remembering them.

  • 04

    The title derives from the nursery rhyme "This little pig went to market..." — each of the five witnesses corresponds in some way to the five pigs, though readers disagree about the precise assignment.

  • 05

    The Cornish setting was inspired by Christie's real affection for Cornwall, where she and Max Mallowan spent much time. The clifftop landscape plays an important role in the novel's atmosphere.

  • 06

    The novel has been praised by literary critics not merely as a detective story but as a serious examination of how love, jealousy, and grief distort memory and the act of witness.
