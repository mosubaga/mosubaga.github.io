Chapter I

About Hercule Poirot

Hercule Poirot is a fictional Belgian detective, one of the most famous characters ever created by Agatha Christie. He appeared in 33 novels, one play, and more than 50 short stories, published between 1920 and 1975. Small in stature but immense in intellect, Poirot is instantly recognisable by his immaculate dress, his magnificent waxed moustaches, and his near-fanatical devotion to order and method.

Born in Belgium, Poirot was once a celebrated member of the Belgian police force before the First World War drove him to England as a refugee. He subsequently established himself as a private detective in London, quickly gaining a reputation as the finest investigator of his age. His success stems not from physical prowess, but from the rigorous application of his “little grey cells” — a favourite phrase suggesting that superior thinking alone can solve any crime.

It is the brain, the little grey cells on which one must rely. One must seek the truth within — not without.

— Hercule Poirot

Christie herself famously described Poirot as a “detestable, bombastic, tiresome, ego-centric little creep” in her diary — yet she could never bring herself to retire him, as public demand for his adventures never waned. He remains one of the best-selling fictional characters of all time, second only to Sherlock Holmes in the pantheon of fictional detectives.

Chapter II

Assistants & Friends

Though Poirot is formidable on his own, he is not without companions who assist him, ground him, and occasionally exasperate him in equal measure.

H

Arthur Hastings

Faithful Companion

Captain Arthur Hastings, OBE, is Poirot's closest friend and frequent chronicler. A cheerful, loyal, and somewhat credulous former soldier, Hastings provides the narrative voice for many of Poirot's early cases. He is the Watson to Poirot's Holmes — warm-hearted but often led astray by a pretty face.

J

Chief Inspector Japp

Scotland Yard

James Japp is Poirot's contact at Scotland Yard. Though he often underestimates Poirot's methods, he comes to rely on the Belgian detective's insights to close his most baffling cases. Bluff, practical, and good-natured, Japp represents the best of conventional police work.

L

Miss Felicity Lemon

Secretary

Miss Lemon is Poirot's supremely efficient secretary, a woman so devoted to order and method that she and Poirot are perfectly matched. Lacking imagination entirely, she is the ideal foil for her employer's flights of intuition, ensuring his professional affairs run like clockwork.

O

Ariadne Oliver

Crime Novelist

A witty, self-deprecating crime novelist clearly modelled on Christie herself, Mrs. Oliver frequently crosses paths with Poirot in later cases. She provides comic relief and sharp insights, while lamenting the fictional Finnish detective she invented and cannot seem to escape.

Chapter III

Hobbies & Pursuits

Despite his all-consuming dedication to crime investigation, Poirot cultivates a rich inner life and a number of refined pastimes that reveal the full depth of his character.

Tisanes & Chocolate

Poirot is an enthusiastic consumer of herbal tisanes and fine chocolate. He has little patience for the English predilection for strong tea and considers a properly made tisane the foundation of clear thinking.

Order & Symmetry

He arranges objects with obsessive precision, aligns books by height, and is genuinely distressed by a crooked picture frame. Order in his surroundings reflects, for him, order in the mind.

The Theatre & Opera

A lover of the arts, Poirot enjoys the theatre and operatic performances. He approaches them with the same analytical attention he gives to a crime scene, appreciating both the craft and the human drama.

Vegetable Marrows

In his brief retirement in the English countryside, Poirot attempts to grow vegetable marrows — with limited horticultural success. It is this very boredom that draws him back into detective work in the events of The Murder of Roger Ackroyd.

Chapter IV

Methods of Investigation

What makes Poirot unique among great detectives is his absolute conviction that the solution to any crime lies not in footprints or tobacco ash, but in the psychology of the criminal. His methods are those of the mind, not the magnifying glass.

01

The Little Grey Cells

Poirot's most famous concept: the supremacy of intellect over physical evidence. By observing what people say, how they say it, and what they omit, he reconstructs the psychological truth of any situation.

02

The Method & The Order

He insists on proceeding with method and order, building his understanding piece by piece. Hasty conclusions and insufficient evidence are anathema to him.

03

Psychology Over Deduction

Unlike Holmes, who reasons from clues outward, Poirot reasons inward — from the character of the victim and suspects to the identity of the killer. His central question is always: what kind of person would commit this crime?

04

The Dénouement

Poirot is famous for gathering all suspects in a room for his theatrical final revelation. This grand summation serves both to unveil the killer and to demonstrate, with devastating clarity, the superiority of his method.

Everything must be taken into account. If the fact will not fit the theory — let the theory go.

— Hercule Poirot
