Murder on the Orient Express

Thirteen suspects. One night. No way off the train.

Synopsis

The Story

Hercule Poirot is travelling home to England aboard the legendary Orient Express when a blizzard strands the train in the Yugoslav mountains. The following morning, a fellow passenger — the boorish, disagreeable American businessman Mr. Samuel Ratchett — is found dead in his locked compartment, stabbed twelve times. There is no way on or off the snowbound train.

With Ratchett's travelling companion M. Bouc — a director of the railway company — pressing for a swift resolution before the police board at the next station, Poirot undertakes a meticulous investigation of all twelve remaining passengers. Each has an alibi. Each has a secret. And each, in some way, is connected to a famous kidnapping case from years past that scandalized America.

The solution Poirot ultimately presents is among the most startling — and morally complex — in all of detective fiction.

Dramatis Personae

The Characters

P

Hercule Poirot

The Investigator

Travelling home from a case in Syria, Poirot finds himself the only man on the train capable of solving this most closed-room of murders. The Orient Express becomes his theatre of deduction.

B

M. Bouc

Railway Director

A director of the Compagnie Internationale des Wagons-Lits and old friend of Poirot. It is Bouc who recruits Poirot to investigate, knowing that the Belgian detective's presence is an extraordinary stroke of fortune.

R

Samuel Ratchett

The Victim

A crude American businessman who had attempted, unsuccessfully, to hire Poirot for his own protection the evening before his death. Ratchett is not quite what he appears to be, and his past conceals a terrible crime.

D

Mary Debenham

English Governess

A composed, self-possessed young Englishwoman returning from a posting in Baghdad. Poirot notes with interest that she seems strangely unrattled by the death — and that she knows the military man Colonel Arbuthnot.

Д

Princess Dragomiroff

Russian Aristocrat

An elderly Russian princess of formidable presence and sharp intelligence. She travels with her maid Schmidt, and her imperious manner conceals a fierce personal loyalty that stretches back many years.

A

Countess Andrenyi

Hungarian Aristocrat

A beautiful young Hungarian countess travelling with her diplomat husband. She appears largely uninvolved in the drama, but her connection to the past case is closer than it first appears.

Trivia
Behind the Book

Trivia & Notes

  • 01

    Christie wrote the novel in two weeks while staying at the Pera Palace Hotel in Istanbul — the very hotel where passengers on the Orient Express would stay. Her room is preserved as a museum exhibit.

  • 02

    The Lindbergh baby kidnapping of 1932 served as the inspiration for the Armstrong kidnapping case that forms the novel's historical backstory.

  • 03

    The Orient Express itself was one of the most glamorous trains in history, running from Paris to Istanbul. Christie travelled on it several times, and it clearly captured her imagination profoundly.

  • 04

    The novel has been adapted for film and television more times than almost any other Christie work, with notable versions starring Albert Finney (1974), David Suchet (2010), and Kenneth Branagh (2017).

  • 05

    The moral dilemma at the heart of the solution was considered so controversial that some readers and critics argued Poirot's final decision was itself a kind of crime. Christie remained characteristically silent on the matter.

  • 06

    Christie dedicated the novel to M.E.L. Mallowan — her second husband, the archaeologist Max Mallowan, whom she had met on a dig in the Middle East.
