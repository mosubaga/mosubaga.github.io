Plain notes, connected thinking

How to use Obsidian for learning, work, and ideas that keep growing.

Obsidian is a note app built around local Markdown files. That sounds simple, but it lets you build a personal knowledge base where class notes, meeting notes, research, code snippets, and project plans connect to each other.

What Obsidian Is

Think of Obsidian as a private folder of notes that becomes more useful as you link ideas together. Your notes live on your device as Markdown files, so you can read and edit them outside the app too.

Local first

Your vault is a folder on your computer or phone. Obsidian does not require an account for basic use, and your notes stay local unless you choose a sync or publishing setup.

Link based

Type [[Note name]] to connect notes. Over time, those links create a map of your thinking instead of a pile of disconnected files.

Searchable

Search, tags, backlinks, properties, and graph view help you find patterns across classes, projects, papers, and meetings.

Extendable

Core plugins cover daily notes, graph view, templates, Canvas, and more. Community plugins can add calendars, diagrams, dashboards, and specialized workflows.

What It Can Help You Do

The main win is not taking prettier notes. The win is being able to reuse what you learned last week, last semester, or during a meeting three projects ago.

School Work Personal research

Study subjects

Build notes for formulas, definitions, examples, proofs, bugs, and solved problems.

Make connections

Connect a physics idea to a math method, or a programming concept to a project you built.

Review actively

Use daily notes, question notes, and spaced review plugins to turn notes into practice.

Meeting memory

Keep agenda, decisions, owners, dates, and follow-ups in one place.

Project context

Link plans, research, people, constraints, and status notes so handoffs are easier.

Reusable knowledge

Turn repeated questions into reference notes, checklists, templates, and playbooks.

Collect sources

Clip articles, store links, and summarize what matters in your own words.

Develop ideas

Use Canvas and links to move from scattered thoughts to an outline or plan.

Own your archive

Because the notes are files, you can back them up, version them, and move them later.

A Beginner Workflow

Use this loop before trying dozens of plugins. It is enough for the first month.

Capture

Put raw notes, ideas, links, meeting notes, and class notes into an Inbox or Daily Notes.

Connect

Add links while the idea is fresh. A good link says, "this reminds me of that."

Organize

Move notes into broad folders, add useful tags, and give important notes properties like status or topic.

Review

Use backlinks, graph view, search, and your weekly review to clean up rough notes and find patterns.

Reuse

Turn notes into essays, study sheets, plans, documentation, or AI prompts grounded in your own material.

Quick Feature Map

These are the Obsidian features this guide keeps returning to. You do not need all of them on day one, but knowing what each one is for makes the app much less mysterious.

  • Links and backlinks: connect notes and see what points back to the current idea.
  • Properties and tags: add structured labels for status, topic, source, or course.
  • Graph view and Canvas: inspect relationships visually when a topic gets crowded.
  • Templates and daily notes: make repeated capture faster and more consistent.
