Study workflows

Use Obsidian to learn ideas, not just store notes.

For school, the goal is active recall, problem solving, and transfer. Obsidian helps when each note becomes a reusable explanation, example, question, or connection.

Subject Patterns

Different subjects need different note shapes. Pick the pattern that matches the work you actually do.

Math

  • Definition notes: one concept, precise wording, examples and non-examples.
  • Proof notes: claim, strategy, key trick, common mistake.
  • Problem notes: problem type, method, solved example, variations.

Physics

  • Concept notes: intuition, equation, units, assumptions.
  • Lab notes: setup, measurement, uncertainty, conclusion.
  • Model links: connect equations to physical situations.

Programming

  • Snippet notes: code, explanation, when to use, edge cases.
  • Error notes: symptom, cause, fix, prevention.
  • Project notes: design decisions, bugs, commands, references.

Make Notes That Quiz You

After class or reading, convert passive notes into questions. This makes review faster and more honest.

Write the question first

Example: "When should I use integration by parts?"

Answer in your own words

Use the textbook only after you try the answer from memory.

Add links

Connect to prerequisite ideas, examples, and common mistakes.

Review from links

Open backlinks and local graph to see what else depends on this idea.

Use Graph View Carefully

Graph view is exciting, but it is most useful when you ask specific questions.

Before an exam

Filter by course tag and look for isolated topics. Orphan notes often mean you memorized details without connecting them.

When stuck

Open local graph from the note you do not understand. Look one or two levels out for missing prerequisites.

For essays

Start from a thesis note, then follow backlinks to evidence, examples, and counterarguments.
