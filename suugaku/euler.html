Euler-Lagrange Equation

The Euler-Lagrange equation turns the idea of a stationary action into a differential equation. It is the bridge from variational principles to Lagrangian mechanics, Hamiltonian mechanics, field theory, geometry, and optimization.

Overview

Instead of describing motion by forces alone, the Euler-Lagrange framework asks which path makes an integral quantity stationary. In mechanics that integral is the action:

\[ S[q] = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} L(q(t), \dot{q}(t), t)\,dt. \]

The true path does not necessarily minimize the action; more precisely, it makes the first variation vanish. This stationary-action viewpoint is powerful because it handles generalized coordinates, constraints, and symmetries naturally.

  • \(q(t)\): generalized coordinate.
  • \(\dot{q}(t)\): generalized velocity.
  • \(L = T - V\): Lagrangian for many mechanical systems.
  • \(S[q]\): action functional.
  • \(\eta(t)\): small endpoint-fixed variation.
  • \(p = \partial L / \partial \dot{q}\): conjugate momentum.

1. Historical Context and Motivation

Newtonian mechanics describes motion through force laws such as \(m\ddot{q}=F\). This is direct and effective, but it can become awkward for constrained systems, non-Cartesian coordinates, and problems where symmetry is the organizing feature.

Euler and Lagrange developed the calculus of variations in the 18th century. In mechanics, their ideas were distilled into Hamilton's principle: among all paths with fixed endpoints, the physical path makes the action stationary.

\[ \delta S = 0. \]

This single condition produces Newton's equations for ordinary particles, geodesic equations in geometry, and field equations in modern physics.

2. The Variational Problem

Let \(y=y(x)\) be differentiable on \([a,b]\). A functional assigns a number to an entire function:

\[ J[y] = \int_a^b L(x, y(x), y'(x))\,dx. \]

The endpoint values are fixed:

Left Endpoint

\[ y(a) = y_a \]

Right Endpoint

\[ y(b) = y_b \]

The goal is to find a function \(y\) for which \(J[y]\) is stationary under all allowed small changes.

3. Variations and the First Variation

Choose an arbitrary smooth variation \(\eta(x)\) that vanishes at the endpoints:

\[ \eta(a)=0, \qquad \eta(b)=0. \]

Perturb the path by a small parameter \(\varepsilon\):

\[ y_\varepsilon(x) = y(x) + \varepsilon \eta(x). \]

Define the ordinary single-variable function \(\Phi(\varepsilon)=J[y_\varepsilon]\). Stationarity of the original functional means

\[ \Phi'(0)=0. \]

Differentiating under the integral sign gives the first variation:

\[ \Phi'(0) = \int_a^b \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial y}\eta + \frac{\partial L}{\partial y'}\eta' \right)\,dx. \]

4. Integration by Parts

The \(\eta'\) term is inconvenient because the variation appears differentiated. Integration by parts moves the derivative onto \(\partial L/\partial y'\):

\[ \int_a^b \frac{\partial L}{\partial y'}\eta'\,dx = \left[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial y'}\eta \right]_a^b - \int_a^b \frac{d}{dx} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial y'} \right)\eta\,dx. \]

The boundary term vanishes because \(\eta(a)=\eta(b)=0\). Therefore

\[ \Phi'(0) = \int_a^b \left[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial y} - \frac{d}{dx} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial y'} \right) \right]\eta\,dx. \]

5. The Euler-Lagrange Equation

Since \(\eta\) is arbitrary inside the interval, the fundamental lemma of the calculus of variations says the coefficient of \(\eta\) must vanish:

\[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial y} - \frac{d}{dx} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial y'} \right) = 0. \]

In mechanics the independent variable is time and \(y\) is replaced by generalized coordinates \(q_i\):

\[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_i} - \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i} \right) = 0, \qquad i=1,\dots,n. \]

These are second-order differential equations for the generalized coordinates.

6. Example: Newton's Law from a Lagrangian

For a particle moving in one dimension with mass \(m\) and potential energy \(V(q)\), use

\[ L(q,\dot{q}) = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{q}^2 - V(q). \]

Position Derivative

\[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial q} = -\frac{dV}{dq} \]

Velocity Derivative

\[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}} = m\dot{q} \]

The Euler-Lagrange equation becomes

\[ -\frac{dV}{dq} - \frac{d}{dt}(m\dot{q}) = 0 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad m\ddot{q} = -\frac{dV}{dq}. \]

The right-hand side is the force \(F=-dV/dq\), so the variational principle recovers Newton's second law.

Why the Equation Is Significant

The Euler-Lagrange equation is significant because it changes the language of mechanics from forces to structure. Once a system's Lagrangian is known, the equations of motion follow mechanically, even in coordinates where Newtonian force bookkeeping would be difficult.

Symmetry gives conservation laws. If \(L\) does not depend on a coordinate \(q_i\), then its conjugate momentum is conserved:

\[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_i}=0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i} \right)=0. \]

This is the gateway to Noether's theorem: continuous symmetries of the action correspond to conserved quantities such as energy, momentum, and angular momentum.

7. From Lagrangian to Hamiltonian Mechanics

Hamiltonian mechanics rewrites the same dynamics using coordinates \(q_i\) and momenta \(p_i\), rather than coordinates and velocities. First define conjugate momentum:

\[ p_i = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}. \]

When the velocity-to-momentum map can be inverted, the Hamiltonian is the Legendre transform of the Lagrangian with respect to the velocities:

\[ H(q,p,t) = \sum_i p_i\dot{q}_i - L(q,\dot{q},t), \]

where \(\dot{q}\) is then rewritten as a function of \((q,p,t)\). This produces Hamilton's equations:

Coordinate Evolution

\[ \dot{q}_i = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i} \]

Momentum Evolution

\[ \dot{p}_i = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q_i} \]

The Euler-Lagrange equation and Hamilton's equations describe the same classical dynamics when the Legendre transform is non-degenerate. The Hamiltonian form is first-order and is especially important in phase-space geometry, canonical transformations, statistical mechanics, and quantum mechanics.

8. What the Legendre Transform Does

A Legendre transform changes which variables are treated as independent. In this setting, it trades velocity \(\dot{q}\) for momentum \(p\). For a simple particle,

\[ L(q,\dot{q}) = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{q}^2 - V(q), \qquad p = m\dot{q}. \]

Solve for \(\dot{q}=p/m\), then compute

\[ H(q,p) = p\left(\frac{p}{m}\right) - \left[ \frac{1}{2}m\left(\frac{p}{m}\right)^2 - V(q) \right] = \frac{p^2}{2m}+V(q). \]

For this common case, \(H=T+V\), the total energy. More generally, the Hamiltonian is the energy-like generator of time evolution in phase space.

Where It Is Used

The same Euler-Lagrange structure appears far beyond introductory mechanics. It gives geodesics in differential geometry, minimal surfaces in geometry and materials science, field equations in classical field theory, and optimization equations in control theory.

In Hamiltonian mechanics, it provides the starting point for passing from configuration space to phase space. In modern physics, this variational foundation is one reason classical mechanics, quantum mechanics, and field theory share a common mathematical grammar.
