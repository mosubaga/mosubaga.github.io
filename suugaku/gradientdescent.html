Gradient Descent & Lagrange Multipliers

Gradient descent descends a function's surface by repeatedly stepping in the direction of steepest decrease. Lagrange multipliers extend this to constrained problems — finding extrema on a surface intersected by a constraint curve or manifold.

Part I — Gradient Descent

Given a differentiable function \(f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}\), the gradient \(\nabla f(\mathbf{x})\) points in the direction of steepest ascent. To minimize \(f\), each update moves opposite to the gradient:

\[ \mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \mathbf{x}_k - \eta \,\nabla f(\mathbf{x}_k), \]

where \(\eta > 0\) is the learning rate (step size). Iterating this rule produces a sequence that, under mild conditions, converges to a local minimum.

Gradient descent on the surface \(f(x,y) = (x-0.3)^2 + 2(y+0.2)^2\). The particle follows the negative-gradient direction at each step, spiraling to the minimum.

1. Why the Negative Gradient?

A first-order Taylor expansion around \(\mathbf{x}_k\) gives the local linear approximation:

\[ f(\mathbf{x}_k + \mathbf{d}) \approx f(\mathbf{x}_k) + \nabla f(\mathbf{x}_k)^\top \mathbf{d}. \]

To decrease \(f\) most rapidly, choose \(\mathbf{d}\) with unit norm so that \(\nabla f^\top \mathbf{d}\) is as negative as possible. The minimum of \(\nabla f^\top \mathbf{d}\) over \(\|\mathbf{d}\|=1\) is \(-\|\nabla f\|\), achieved at \(\mathbf{d} = -\nabla f / \|\nabla f\|\). Scaling by step size \(\eta\) gives the gradient-descent update.

2. Choosing the Learning Rate

The learning rate \(\eta\) controls the trade-off between speed and stability.

Too small

Convergence is safe but very slow; many iterations are needed to move a meaningful distance.

Too large

Updates overshoot the minimum; the loss can oscillate or diverge.

For a quadratic \(f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{x}^\top A \mathbf{x}\) with symmetric positive-definite \(A\), gradient descent converges provided

\[ 0 < \eta < \frac{2}{\lambda_{\max}(A)}, \]

where \(\lambda_{\max}(A)\) is the largest eigenvalue of \(A\). The optimal (fastest) fixed step size is \(\eta^* = 2/(\lambda_{\min} + \lambda_{\max})\).

3. Variants: Batch, Stochastic, and Mini-batch

In machine learning, the objective is typically an average over a training set:

\[ f(\theta) = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^{m} \ell(\theta;\, x_i, y_i). \]

Batch GD

Uses the full dataset to compute an exact gradient each step. Stable but expensive for large \(m\).

Stochastic GD (SGD)

Updates with a single randomly chosen sample at each step. Fast per iteration but noisy.

Mini-batch GD

A batch of \(b \ll m\) samples; balances speed and gradient accuracy. Standard in deep learning.

Adam / Momentum

Adaptive methods maintain a running average of gradients and squared gradients, accelerating convergence on ill-conditioned surfaces.

4. Worked Example — Minimizing a Quadratic

Let \(f(x,y) = (x-1)^2 + 5(y-2)^2\). The gradient is

\[ \nabla f(x,y) = \bigl(2(x-1),\; 10(y-2)\bigr). \]

Starting from \((x_0, y_0) = (4, 5)\) with \(\eta = 0.1\), the first update is

\[ \begin{pmatrix}x_1\\y_1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}4\\5\end{pmatrix} - 0.1 \begin{pmatrix}6\\30\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}3.4\\2\end{pmatrix}. \]

After each step the iterates move toward the true minimum \((1,2)\). The \(y\)-component converges faster because the curvature (5 vs 1) is higher.

Part II — Lagrange Multipliers

Many real optimization problems constrain the search space. The method of Lagrange multipliers solves

\[ \min_{\mathbf{x}} f(\mathbf{x}) \quad \text{subject to} \quad g_i(\mathbf{x}) = 0, \quad i = 1, \ldots, k. \]

At a constrained extremum, the gradient of \(f\) must be a linear combination of the constraint gradients:

\[ \nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*) = \sum_{i=1}^k \lambda_i \,\nabla g_i(\mathbf{x}^*). \]

The scalars \(\lambda_i\) are the Lagrange multipliers. Together with the constraint equations, these conditions form a system that can be solved for both \(\mathbf{x}^*\) and the multipliers.

5. Geometric Intuition

Consider a single constraint \(g(\mathbf{x}) = 0\) defining a curve in \(\mathbb{R}^2\). Level curves of \(f\) are the sets where \(f\) is constant. An extremum on the constraint occurs where a level curve of \(f\) is tangent to the constraint curve.

At a tangency point, the normals to both curves are parallel, meaning their gradients are parallel:

\[ \nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*) = \lambda \,\nabla g(\mathbf{x}^*). \]

If the gradients were not parallel, one could move along the constraint to a point with a lower value of \(f\), contradicting the assumption of a constrained minimum.

6. The Lagrangian and the System of Equations

The standard procedure encodes both the objective and constraints into a single function called the Lagrangian:

\[ \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}, \boldsymbol{\lambda}) = f(\mathbf{x}) - \sum_{i=1}^k \lambda_i \, g_i(\mathbf{x}). \]

Setting all partial derivatives of \(\mathcal{L}\) to zero gives the necessary conditions:

Stationarity in \(\mathbf{x}\)

\[ \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{x}} = 0 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \nabla f = \lambda \nabla g \]

Primal feasibility

\[ \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \lambda_i} = 0 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; g_i(\mathbf{x}) = 0 \]

This is a system of \(n + k\) equations in the unknowns \((\mathbf{x}, \boldsymbol{\lambda})\). The Lagrange multiplier \(\lambda_i\) measures how much the optimal objective value would change if the \(i\)-th constraint were relaxed slightly.

7. Worked Example — Maximize Area with Fixed Perimeter

Among all rectangles with perimeter \(2s\), which has the largest area? Let the sides be \(x\) and \(y\). We maximize \(f(x,y) = xy\) subject to \(g(x,y) = x + y - s = 0\).

Form the Lagrangian \(\mathcal{L} = xy - \lambda(x + y - s)\). Setting partial derivatives to zero:

\[ \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x} = y - \lambda = 0, \qquad \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial y} = x - \lambda = 0, \qquad x + y = s. \]

The first two equations give \(y = \lambda = x\), so \(x = y = s/2\). The optimal rectangle is a square with side \(s/2\) and area \(s^2/4\).

Interpretation of \(\lambda\). Here \(\lambda = s/2\). The objective \(xy\) evaluated at the optimum equals \(s^2/4\). If the perimeter constraint is relaxed by \(\varepsilon\) (replacing \(s\) with \(s+\varepsilon\)), the new optimal area changes by approximately \(\lambda \cdot \varepsilon = (s/2)\varepsilon\), which matches the derivative of \((s+\varepsilon)^2/4\) with respect to \(\varepsilon\).

8. Inequality Constraints — KKT Conditions

When constraints are inequalities \(g_i(\mathbf{x}) \le 0\), the necessary conditions for optimality are the Karush–Kuhn–Tucker (KKT) conditions:

\[ \nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*) + \sum_i \mu_i \nabla g_i(\mathbf{x}^*) = 0, \qquad \mu_i \ge 0, \qquad \mu_i \, g_i(\mathbf{x}^*) = 0. \]

The complementary slackness condition \(\mu_i g_i = 0\) says that a multiplier is positive only when its constraint is active (binding). KKT conditions reduce to Lagrange conditions when all constraints are equalities. They underpin support-vector machines, constrained neural network training, and convex programming duality.

Connection Between the Two Methods

Gradient descent and Lagrange multipliers attack the same fundamental question — where does a function stop decreasing? — but in different regimes.

  • Unconstrained minimization (gradient descent): iterate \(\mathbf{x} \leftarrow \mathbf{x} - \eta \nabla f(\mathbf{x})\) until \(\nabla f = 0\).
  • Constrained minimization (Lagrange): find the point where \(\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g\) and \(g = 0\) simultaneously.

The two are unified in the augmented Lagrangian method and projected gradient descent. In projected gradient descent, each gradient step is followed by a projection back onto the feasible set \(\{g = 0\}\). In the augmented Lagrangian method, gradient descent is applied to

\[ \mathcal{L}_\rho(\mathbf{x}, \lambda) = f(\mathbf{x}) - \lambda g(\mathbf{x}) + \frac{\rho}{2}g(\mathbf{x})^2, \]

alternating between updating \(\mathbf{x}\) by gradient steps and updating \(\lambda\) by dual ascent. This is the basis of the ADMM algorithm used throughout large-scale optimization.

9. Real-World Applications

  • Deep Learning. SGD and its variants (Adam, RMSProp) minimize cross-entropy or mean-squared-error loss over neural-network parameters. Lagrange-inspired regularization (weight decay) can be viewed as a soft constraint on parameter norms.
  • Support Vector Machines. The SVM margin maximization is a quadratic program solved exactly via KKT conditions. The support vectors are the points where inequality constraints are active (\(\mu_i > 0\)).
  • Portfolio Optimization. Markowitz mean-variance optimization minimizes portfolio variance subject to a target return constraint, solved by Lagrange multipliers. The multiplier equals the marginal cost of achieving a higher return.
  • Physics. Constrained mechanical systems (pendulums, rigid bodies) use Lagrange multipliers as constraint forces. In thermodynamics, entropy maximization subject to energy and particle-number constraints yields the Boltzmann distribution, with \(\beta = 1/k_BT\) as the multiplier.
  • Economics. Utility maximization subject to a budget constraint is solved by Lagrange multipliers. The multiplier is the marginal utility of income — how much additional utility one unit of currency buys at the optimum.
