Law of Total Probability

The law of total probability is a way of computing the probability of an event by splitting the sample space into cases. Instead of trying to measure the event all at once, we condition on a set of mutually exclusive and exhaustive events, solve each case, and then add the weighted results.

A partition of the sample space: each case contributes \(P(A \mid B_i)P(B_i)\) to the total.

If \(B_1, B_2, \ldots, B_n\) form a partition of the sample space and \(P(B_i) > 0\), then for any event \(A\),

\[ P(A) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(A \mid B_i)P(B_i). \]

The formula says: first ask which case \(B_i\) happened, then ask how likely \(A\) is inside that case. The final probability is the average of those conditional probabilities, weighted by how likely each case is.

A Small Example

Suppose a bag is chosen at random. Bag 1 is chosen with probability \(0.4\) and contains red balls with probability \(0.7\). Bag 2 is chosen with probability \(0.6\) and contains red balls with probability \(0.2\). If \(A\) is the event "draw a red ball", then

\(P(A) = P(A\mid B_1)P(B_1) + P(A\mid B_2)P(B_2)\)
\(P(A) = (0.7)(0.4) + (0.2)(0.6) = 0.40.\)

The key move is not the arithmetic. It is choosing a useful partition. In this example, the partition is "which bag was selected." In many stochastic-process problems, the useful partition is "what happens on the next step."

First-Step Analysis

First-step analysis is the law of total probability applied to a process that evolves one step at a time. To compute the probability of a future event, condition on the first transition. After that first transition, the process starts again from a new state.

If \(u_i\) denotes the probability of eventually reaching a target state when starting from state \(i\), then conditioning on the next state gives

\[ u_i = \sum_j P(i \to j)u_j. \]

This is total probability: the next state \(j\) partitions all possible first moves, and \(u_j\) is the conditional probability of eventual success after that first move.

Gambler's Ruin

In the classical gambler's ruin problem, a gambler starts with \(i\) dollars and plays repeated independent bets. On each bet the gambler wins one dollar with probability \(p\) and loses one dollar with probability \(q = 1-p\). The game stops when the gambler reaches either \(0\) dollars or \(N\) dollars.

Let \(u_i\) be the probability that the gambler reaches \(N\) before reaching \(0\), starting from \(i\). The boundary values are immediate:

\[ u_0 = 0,\qquad u_N = 1. \]

For \(1 \leq i \leq N-1\), condition on the outcome of the next bet. If the gambler wins, the new fortune is \(i+1\). If the gambler loses, the new fortune is \(i-1\). By the law of total probability,

\[ u_i = p\,u_{i+1} + q\,u_{i-1}. \]

This recurrence is the central connection. The law of total probability turns the question "what is the chance of eventual success?" into a relationship between neighboring states.

Solving the Recurrence

When \(p \neq q\), the solution to \(u_i = p\,u_{i+1} + q\,u_{i-1}\) with \(u_0=0\) and \(u_N=1\) is

\[ u_i = \frac{1 - \left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^i} {1 - \left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^N}. \]

When the game is fair, \(p=q=\tfrac{1}{2}\), the recurrence becomes \(u_i = \tfrac{1}{2}u_{i+1} + \tfrac{1}{2}u_{i-1}\). The solution is linear:

\[ u_i = \frac{i}{N}. \]

Interpretation

In a fair game, the probability of reaching \(N\) before \(0\) is exactly the fraction of the way the gambler has already traveled toward \(N\). Starting with \(3\) dollars and aiming for \(10\), the chance of success is \(3/10\).

In an unfair game, the ratio \(q/p\) controls the shape of the answer. If \(p < q\), then losses are more likely than wins, and the chance of reaching \(N\) decreases sharply. If \(p > q\), the recurrence bends in the gambler's favor.

Why Total Probability Matters Here

Gambler's ruin is not solved by listing every possible sequence of wins and losses. There are infinitely many possible paths before absorption. The law of total probability avoids that enumeration by grouping paths according to the first bet. Once the first bet is known, the remaining problem has the same form as the original problem, only from a neighboring fortune.

The pattern is useful far beyond gambling examples:

  • Condition on the next step.
  • Write the target probability in terms of neighboring states.
  • Use boundary conditions to solve the recurrence.
