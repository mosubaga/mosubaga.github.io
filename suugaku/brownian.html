Brownian Motion

A large particle (orange) buffeted by many small fluid molecules (blue). Its erratic trail illustrates the hallmark of Brownian motion.

1. Historical background

In 1827, the Scottish botanist Robert Brown observed under his microscope that pollen grains suspended in water moved in rapid, irregular, seemingly endless agitation. He initially wondered whether the motion was a sign of life, but found the same jittering in inorganic dust particles and even in ground-up sphinx fragments millennia old. The phenomenon is now called Brownian motion in his honor.

The physical explanation had to wait until 1905, when Albert Einstein published a landmark paper showing that Brownian motion is a direct consequence of the molecular-kinetic theory of heat: a suspended particle is incessantly bombarded by the surrounding fluid molecules, each collision delivering a random impulse. Because the impacts are individually tiny yet overwhelmingly numerous, the net effect on the particle is a random walk whose statistical properties can be predicted with precision. The same year, Marian Smoluchowski independently arrived at related results. Jean Perrin's meticulous experiments (1908–1909) confirmed Einstein's formulae and provided one of the strongest early confirmations of the atomic hypothesis, earning Perrin the 1926 Nobel Prize in Physics.

2. The random-walk model

The simplest model considers a particle making discrete steps in one dimension. At each time step \(\Delta t\) the particle moves by \(+\ell\) or \(-\ell\) with equal probability \(\tfrac{1}{2}\). After \(N\) steps the position is

\begin{equation} X_N = \ell \sum_{i=1}^{N} \xi_i, \end{equation}

where \(\xi_i \in \{-1,+1\}\) are independent, identically distributed random variables with \(\mathbb{E}[\xi_i]=0\) and \(\mathbb{E}[\xi_i^2]=1\). Because the steps are independent and have zero mean,

\begin{equation} \mathbb{E}[X_N] = 0, \qquad \mathbb{E}[X_N^2] = N\ell^2. \end{equation}

So while the average displacement is zero, the root-mean-square displacement grows as \(\sqrt{N}\). Setting \(t = N\Delta t\) and \(D = \ell^2 / (2\Delta t)\), this becomes

\begin{equation} \mathbb{E}[X^2] = 2Dt, \end{equation}

the celebrated diffusive scaling law. The quantity \(D\) is called the diffusion coefficient.

3. The Wiener process

Taking the continuum limit — step size \(\ell \to 0\) and step duration \(\Delta t \to 0\) with \(D\) held fixed — one obtains the Wiener process \((W_t)_{t \geq 0}\), which is the rigorous mathematical model for Brownian motion. It is characterized by four properties:

  1. \(W_0 = 0\) almost surely.
  2. The increments \(W_t - W_s\) are independent of \(\mathcal{F}_s\) whenever \(s < t\) (independent increments).
  3. For \(s < t\), the increment \(W_t - W_s \sim \mathcal{N}(0, t-s)\).
  4. The sample paths \(t \mapsto W_t\) are continuous almost surely.

A remarkable consequence of property (3) and the law of the iterated logarithm is that while continuous, the Wiener process is nowhere differentiable with probability one — reflecting the physically unbounded, instantaneous "velocity" of a Brownian particle.

4. Einstein's diffusion equation

Einstein derived the evolution of the probability density \(p(x,t)\) of finding the particle at position \(x\) at time \(t\) from the assumption that the displacements in a short interval \(\tau\) are governed by a symmetric distribution \(\phi(\Delta)\). Expanding in a Taylor series he obtained the diffusion equation

\begin{equation} \frac{\partial p}{\partial t} = D\,\frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial x^2}. \end{equation}

With the initial condition \(p(x,0)=\delta(x)\) (particle starts at the origin), the solution is a Gaussian that spreads over time:

\begin{equation} p(x,t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi D t}}\exp\!\left(-\frac{x^2}{4Dt}\right). \end{equation}

One immediately recovers \(\mathbb{E}[X^2]=2Dt\) by computing the variance of this Gaussian. In \(d\) spatial dimensions the mean-squared displacement becomes \(\mathbb{E}[|\mathbf{X}|^2] = 2dDt\).

5. The Einstein–Smoluchowski–Stokes relation

Einstein connected \(D\) to measurable physical quantities. For a spherical particle of radius \(r\) in a fluid of viscosity \(\eta\) at absolute temperature \(T\), Stokes's law gives the drag coefficient \(\gamma = 6\pi\eta r\), and Einstein's relation reads

\begin{equation} D = \frac{k_{\mathrm{B}} T}{\gamma} = \frac{k_{\mathrm{B}} T}{6\pi\eta r}, \end{equation}

where \(k_{\mathrm{B}}\) is Boltzmann's constant. This is one instance of the more general fluctuation–dissipation theorem: the same thermal fluctuations that drive the random walk also cause the viscous resistance, and the two are related by temperature. By measuring \(D\) experimentally (from \(\mathbb{E}[X^2]=2Dt\)) and knowing \(\eta\) and \(r\), Perrin was able to determine \(k_{\mathrm{B}}\) and thereby Avogadro's number \(N_A = R/k_{\mathrm{B}}\) with remarkable accuracy.

6. Itô calculus and stochastic differential equations

The Wiener process forms the foundation of modern stochastic calculus. Because Brownian sample paths have infinite variation, ordinary Riemann–Stieltjes integration cannot be used to define integrals driven by \(W_t\). Kiyosi Itô (1944) introduced a stochastic integral that instead uses the non-anticipating (adapted) structure of the integrand, giving rise to the Itô integral

\begin{equation} \int_0^t f(s)\,dW_s = \lim_{n\to\infty} \sum_{k} f(t_k)\bigl(W_{t_{k+1}}-W_{t_k}\bigr). \end{equation}

A key consequence is Itô's lemma: if \(X_t\) satisfies the stochastic differential equation \(dX_t = \mu_t\,dt + \sigma_t\,dW_t\), then for a smooth function \(f(t,x)\),

\begin{equation} df(t,X_t) = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial t} + \mu_t \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} + \frac{\sigma_t^2}{2}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2}\right)dt + \sigma_t\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\,dW_t. \end{equation}

The extra second-derivative term — absent in ordinary calculus — is the signature of the quadratic variation \([W]_t = t\) of Brownian motion, capturing the rough, nowhere-differentiable nature of the sample paths. Itô calculus is the backbone of modern mathematical finance, filtering theory, and the study of stochastic partial differential equations.
