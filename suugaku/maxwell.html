Maxwell's Equations and Their Tensor Form

Maxwell's equations unify electricity, magnetism, and light into one coherent theory. This page gives the four classical laws, the meaning of each term, the wave equation, and the compact relativistic tensor notation.

Overview

Maxwell's equations describe how electric fields \(\mathbf{E}\), magnetic fields \(\mathbf{B}\), charges, and currents interact. Their power is that local field rules also predict a global phenomenon: self-sustaining electromagnetic waves that travel at the speed of light.

  • \(\rho\): electric charge density.
  • \(\mathbf{J}\): electric current density.
  • \(\epsilon_0\): vacuum permittivity.
  • \(\mu_0\): vacuum permeability.
  • \(\Phi_E\): electric flux through a surface.
  • \(\Phi_B\): magnetic flux through a surface.

A p5.js sketch of a plane electromagnetic wave: the electric field and magnetic field oscillate at right angles, and the combined wave moves in the direction of energy flow.

1. Gauss's Law for Electric Fields

Electric charge is the source of electric flux. A closed surface with more net charge inside it has more electric field passing through it.

Differential Form

\[ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0} \]

Integral Form

\[ \oint_{S} \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{a} = \frac{q_{\mathrm{enc}}}{\epsilon_0} \]

The differential form is local: it says what happens at a point. The integral form is global: it adds the flux over an entire closed surface.

2. Gauss's Law for Magnetism

Magnetic field lines form loops rather than beginning or ending on isolated magnetic charges. In the classical theory, there are no magnetic monopoles.

Differential Form

\[ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0 \]

Integral Form

\[ \oint_{S} \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{a} = 0 \]

Any magnetic field line entering a closed surface must also leave it, so the net magnetic flux is zero.

3. Faraday's Law of Induction

A changing magnetic field creates a circulating electric field. This is the mathematical heart of induction, generators, and transformers.

Differential Form

\[ \nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \]

Integral Form

\[ \oint_{C} \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} \]

The minus sign is Lenz's law: the induced effect opposes the change in magnetic flux that produced it.

4. The Ampere-Maxwell Law

Magnetic fields are produced by electric currents and by changing electric fields. Maxwell's added term, the displacement current, makes the equation consistent with charge conservation.

Differential Form

\[ \nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J} + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t} \]

Integral Form

\[ \oint_{C} \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 I_{\mathrm{enc}} + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{d\Phi_E}{dt} \]

Without the displacement-current term, the original Ampere law fails in time-dependent settings, such as the gap between the plates of a charging capacitor.

Maxwell's Correction and the Birth of Electromagnetic Waves

In empty space, where \(\rho = 0\) and \(\mathbf{J} = \mathbf{0}\), the four equations reduce to a coupled set for \(\mathbf{E}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\). Taking the curl of Faraday's law and using the Ampere-Maxwell law gives the wave equations

\[ \nabla^2 \mathbf{E} = \mu_0\epsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2}, \qquad \nabla^2 \mathbf{B} = \mu_0\epsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{B}}{\partial t^2}. \]

These have wave speed

\[ c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0\epsilon_0}}. \]

Because this speed matches the measured speed of light, Maxwell concluded that light is an electromagnetic wave. The electric and magnetic fields regenerate each other as the wave propagates.

Maxwell's Equations in Matter

In materials, microscopic charges inside atoms polarize and magnetize. It is often more useful to work with the auxiliary fields \(\mathbf{D}\) and \(\mathbf{H}\), which separate free sources from bound material response:

\[ \begin{aligned} \nabla \cdot \mathbf{D} &= \rho_{\mathrm{free}},\\ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} &= 0,\\ \nabla \times \mathbf{E} &= -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t},\\ \nabla \times \mathbf{H} &= \mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{free}} + \frac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t}. \end{aligned} \]

For simple linear materials, one often writes \(\mathbf{D} = \epsilon\mathbf{E}\) and \(\mathbf{B} = \mu\mathbf{H}\), but real materials can be nonlinear, anisotropic, or dispersive.

1. The Current 4-Vector \(J^\mu\)

Special relativity combines charge density and current density into the 4-current:

\[ J^\mu = (c\rho,\; J_x,\; J_y,\; J_z). \]

Charge conservation becomes the compact continuity equation

\[ \partial_\mu J^\mu = 0. \]

2. The Electromagnetic Field Tensor \(F^{\mu\nu}\)

The electric and magnetic fields combine into an antisymmetric tensor called the Faraday tensor. In terms of the 4-potential \(A^\mu = (V/c,\mathbf{A})\),

\[ F_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu A_\nu - \partial_\nu A_\mu. \]

One common convention writes the contravariant tensor as

\[ F^{\mu\nu} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -E_x/c & -E_y/c & -E_z/c \\ E_x/c & 0 & -B_z & B_y \\ E_y/c & B_z & 0 & -B_x \\ E_z/c & -B_y & B_x & 0 \end{pmatrix}. \]

The diagonal entries vanish and \(F^{\mu\nu} = -F^{\nu\mu}\). Different sign conventions occur in the literature because authors choose different metric signatures and index placements.

3. Maxwell's Equations in Tensor Form

The four classical equations become two compact tensor equations:

\[ \partial_\nu F^{\mu\nu} = \mu_0 J^\mu \]

This inhomogeneous equation contains Gauss's law for electricity and the Ampere-Maxwell law.

\[ \partial_\lambda F_{\mu\nu} + \partial_\mu F_{\nu\lambda} + \partial_\nu F_{\lambda\mu} = 0. \]

This homogeneous equation contains Gauss's law for magnetism and Faraday's law. Using the dual tensor \(\tilde{F}^{\mu\nu}\), it is equivalently written as

\[ \partial_\nu \tilde{F}^{\mu\nu} = 0. \]

4. Potential, Gauge Invariance, and the Lorenz Gauge

Potentials are not unique. The fields are unchanged under the gauge transformation

\[ A_\mu \longrightarrow A_\mu + \partial_\mu \chi, \]

for a scalar function \(\chi\). In the Lorenz gauge,

\[ \partial_\mu A^\mu = 0, \]

Maxwell's equations reduce to a wave equation for the 4-potential:

\[ \Box A^\mu = \mu_0 J^\mu, \qquad \Box = \nabla^2 - \frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial t^2}. \]

This formulation shows why electromagnetism fits so naturally into spacetime: electric and magnetic fields are frame-dependent parts of a single field tensor.

Physical Significance

Maxwell's equations replace action-at-a-distance with field dynamics. Charges and currents shape fields; fields store energy and momentum; and changing fields travel through empty space as waves.

The tensor formulation makes the unification sharper: what one observer describes as a purely electric field, another moving observer may describe as a mixture of electric and magnetic fields. Electricity and magnetism are different perspectives on one electromagnetic field.
