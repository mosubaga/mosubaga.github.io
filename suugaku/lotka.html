Differential Equations Notes

Lotka–Volterra Equations

A pair of nonlinear first-order ODEs that model the cyclic dynamics of two interacting populations — a prey species and a predator species — in a closed ecosystem.

1. The Equations

Let \(x(t)\) denote the prey (e.g. rabbits) and \(y(t)\) the predator (e.g. foxes). The Lotka–Volterra system is

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{dx}{dt} &= \alpha\, x - \beta\, x y, \\[6pt] \frac{dy}{dt} &= \delta\, x y - \gamma\, y. \end{aligned} \]

Both equations are nonlinear because of the interaction term \(xy\), which couples the two populations. This coupling is the engine of the oscillation.

  • \(x(t) \ge 0\): prey population density.
  • \(y(t) \ge 0\): predator population density.
  • \(\alpha > 0\): intrinsic prey growth rate.
  • \(\beta > 0\): predation rate coefficient.
  • \(\delta > 0\): predator growth per prey consumed.
  • \(\gamma > 0\): intrinsic predator death rate.

2. Historical Context

Alfred Lotka proposed the equations in 1910 (chemistry) and again in 1920 (ecology). Independently, Vito Volterra derived the same system in 1926 to explain the surprising increase of adriatic shark catches after World War I (when fishing was interrupted). The model confirmed that reducing harvesting pressure on prey inadvertently boosted predator numbers.

3. Modelling Assumptions and Derivation

Prey equation. In isolation prey grow exponentially:

\[\frac{dx}{dt} = \alpha\, x.\]

Each encounter between a predator and a prey (rate \(\propto xy\)) removes a prey individual, so the full prey equation becomes

\[\frac{dx}{dt} = \alpha\, x - \beta\, x y.\]

Predator equation. In isolation predators die exponentially:

\[\frac{dy}{dt} = -\gamma\, y.\]

Each encounter also feeds a predator; a fraction \(\delta\) of kills converts into new predators, giving

\[\frac{dy}{dt} = \delta\, x y - \gamma\, y.\]

4. Equilibria and Stability

Setting both right-hand sides to zero gives two fixed points:

Trivial equilibrium

\[E_0 = (0,\,0)\]

Both populations extinct. A saddle point — unstable in the biologically relevant quadrant \(x,y>0\).

Coexistence equilibrium

\[E^* = \!\left(\frac{\gamma}{\delta},\;\frac{\alpha}{\beta}\right)\]

Both species coexist. All trajectories starting in \(x,y>0\) orbit around \(E^*\) periodically — it is a centre, not a spiral.

Linearising around \(E^*\) yields eigenvalues \(\pm i\sqrt{\alpha\gamma}\) (purely imaginary), confirming the centre character at the linearised level. The full nonlinear system is also a centre, proved by the conserved quantity below.

5. First Integral (Conservation Law)

Despite being nonlinear, the Lotka–Volterra system admits a conserved quantity \(V(x,y)\) that is constant along every trajectory:

\[ V(x,y) = \delta\, x - \gamma \ln x + \beta\, y - \alpha \ln y = \text{const}. \]

Proof sketch. Divide the prey equation by \(x\) and the predator equation by \(y\):

\[ \frac{\dot x}{x} = \alpha - \beta y, \qquad \frac{\dot y}{y} = \delta x - \gamma. \]

Then compute \(\dot V\):

\[ \frac{dV}{dt} = \delta\dot x - \frac{\gamma \dot x}{x} + \beta \dot y - \frac{\alpha \dot y}{y} = (\delta - \tfrac{\gamma}{x})(\alpha x - \beta xy) + (\beta - \tfrac{\alpha}{y})(\delta xy - \gamma y) = 0. \]

Because \(V\) is conserved and strictly convex with a unique minimum at \(E^*\), the level curves of \(V\) are closed loops around \(E^*\), which are precisely the orbits of the system — confirming the exact periodicity.

Period. There is no closed-form expression for the period \(T\) of a general orbit. For orbits near \(E^*\) the linearised period is \(T \approx 2\pi / \sqrt{\alpha\gamma}\), independent of \(\beta\) and \(\delta\).

7. Numerical Solution via RK4

Because the Lotka–Volterra system has no explicit closed-form solution for \(x(t)\) and \(y(t)\) individually, we integrate numerically. The classical fourth-order Runge–Kutta scheme advances one step from \((x_n, y_n)\) at time \(t_n\) to \(t_{n+1} = t_n + h\):

\[ \mathbf{u}_n = \begin{pmatrix} x_n \\ y_n \end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{u}) = \begin{pmatrix} \alpha x - \beta xy \\ \delta xy - \gamma y \end{pmatrix}. \]
\[ \begin{aligned} \mathbf{k}_1 &= \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{u}_n), \\ \mathbf{k}_2 &= \mathbf{f}\!\left(\mathbf{u}_n + \tfrac{h}{2}\mathbf{k}_1\right), \\ \mathbf{k}_3 &= \mathbf{f}\!\left(\mathbf{u}_n + \tfrac{h}{2}\mathbf{k}_2\right), \\ \mathbf{k}_4 &= \mathbf{f}\!\left(\mathbf{u}_n + h\,\mathbf{k}_3\right), \\[6pt] \mathbf{u}_{n+1} &= \mathbf{u}_n + \frac{h}{6}\!\left(\mathbf{k}_1 + 2\mathbf{k}_2 + 2\mathbf{k}_3 + \mathbf{k}_4\right). \end{aligned} \]

The local truncation error is \(O(h^5)\) and the global error \(O(h^4)\), making RK4 well suited for smooth ODEs like this one. The conserved quantity \(V\) stays nearly constant over long integrations (typical drift \(\lesssim 10^{-4}\) for \(h=0.02\)), confirming the solution quality.

8. Limitations of the Model

The classic Lotka–Volterra model is a beautiful mathematical object but an idealised ecological one. Key simplifications:

  • No carrying capacity. Prey grow without bound in the absence of predators. Adding a logistic term \(-rx^2\) converts the centre into a stable spiral.
  • Mass-action interaction. The term \(xy\) assumes random mixing; real ecosystems have spatial structure and predator saturation (Holling functional responses).
  • Neutral stability. Every orbit is neutrally stable — a tiny perturbation shifts to a different closed orbit permanently. Real populations show damped oscillations toward a stable limit cycle.
  • Two-species only. Real food webs involve many interacting species.

Despite these limitations, the Lotka–Volterra equations remain the canonical entry point for population dynamics and have influenced fields from epidemiology to game theory.
