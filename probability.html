Introduction to Probability

1.1 The Science of Uncertainty

Probability theory is a mathematical discipline that applies abstract, logical-deductive reasoning to formalize the rules of chance. While the concepts of uncertainty are as old as civilization, the formal field developed relatively late compared to geometry or algebra.

Historical Context

The birth of modern probability is traditionally attributed to a 17th-century correspondence between Blaise Pascal and Pierre de Fermat regarding games of chance. Earlier contributions were made by Gerolamo Cardano, who wrote the first book on gambling around 1584, and Galileo Galilei. The field underwent a major transformation in the 20th century when Andrei Kolmogorov established a rigorous axiomatic foundation, placing probability firmly within the realm of modern mathematics.

Probability vs. Statistics

While closely related, these two fields approach uncertainty from different directions:

  • Probability Theory: Starts with known models and axioms to determine the likelihood of various outcomes.
  • Statistics: The "art of learning from data". It uses observations to infer the underlying probability models or population characteristics.

1.2 Experiments, Sample Spaces, and Events

To analyze chance mathematically, we must define the framework of a random experiment.

  • Experiment: Any process, real or hypothetical, with outcomes that can be identified in advance but cannot be predicted with certainty.
  • Sample Space (\(S\) or \(\Omega\)): The set of all possible outcomes of an experiment.
    • Example: Tossing a coin twice results in the sample space \(S = \{HH, HT, TH, TT\}\).
  • Event: Any subset of the sample space. An event occurs if the actual outcome of the experiment is a member of that subset.
    • Special Events: The sure event (\(S\) itself) is certain to occur, while the impossible event (the empty set \(\emptyset\)) can never occur.

1.3 Interpretations of Probability

There are three primary ways to interpret what a "probability" actually represents:

  1. Classical Interpretation: Based on the assumption of equally likely outcomes. The probability of an event is the ratio of favorable cases to the total number of possible cases in the sample space.
  2. Frequentist Interpretation: Views probability as the limiting relative frequency of an event. If an experiment is repeated \(n\) times and the event occurs \(h\) times, the probability is approximately \(h/n\) for very large \(n\).
  3. Subjective (Bayesian) Interpretation: Defines probability as a measure of an individual's degree of belief or conviction regarding the occurrence of a unique, non-repeatable event.

1.4 Kolmogorov’s Axioms

Regardless of the interpretation used, all probability measures must satisfy three fundamental axioms established by Kolmogorov:

  • Axiom 1 (Non-negativity): For any event \(E\), \(0 \leq P(E) \leq 1\).
  • Axiom 2 (Certainty): The probability of the entire sample space is 1, \(P(S) = 1\).
  • Axiom 3 (Additivity): For any sequence of mutually exclusive events (events that cannot happen at the same time), the probability of their union is the sum of their individual probabilities.

1.5 The Algebra of Events

Logical relations between events are expressed using set theory. Venn diagrams are often used to visualize these relationships.

  • Union (\(A \cup B\)): The event that "either \(A\) or \(B\) or both" occur.
  • Intersection (\(A \cap B\)): The event that "both \(A\) and \(B\)" occur.
  • Complement (\(A^c\)): The event that "\(A\) does not occur".
  • DeMorgan’s Laws: Useful identities for simplifying complex event relations:
    1. \((A \cup B)^c = A^c \cap B^c\)
    2. \((A \cap B)^c = A^c \cup B^c\)

1.6 Principles of Counting (Combinatorics)

In many experiments where outcomes are equally likely, calculating probability reduces to a problem of counting.

  • The Basic Principle of Counting (Multiplication Rule): If one task can result in \(m\) outcomes and a second task in \(n\) outcomes, there are \(m \times n\) total possible outcomes for the two tasks combined.
  • Permutations (\(P_{n,k}\)): The number of ways to arrange \(k\) items from a set of \(n\) when order matters.
  • Combinations (\(n\) choose \(k\)): The number of ways to select a subset of \(k\) items from a set of \(n\) when order does not matter. This is expressed as:

    \[ \binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!} \]
