Stochastic Calculus in Soft Motion

From Brownian Motion to the Ito Integral

Stochastic calculus extends classical calculus to random motion, giving a toolkit to model noisy systems in finance, physics, and biology.

1) Brownian Motion: The Core Random Process

A standard Brownian motion $W_t$ starts at zero, has independent increments, and each increment is normally distributed with variance equal to the time step. It is continuous but nowhere differentiable, which is why classical derivatives fail.

$$W_0 = 0, \quad W_t - W_s \sim \mathcal{N}(0, t-s)$$
Independent increments Disjoint time intervals produce independent random changes, making Brownian motion memoryless.
Gaussian steps Each increment is normal with mean 0 and variance proportional to elapsed time.
Continuous paths Paths are continuous, but the wiggles are so rough that the usual derivative does not exist.
Scaling rule $W_{ct}$ has the same distribution as $\sqrt{c} W_t$ for any $c > 0$.

2) The Ito Integral: Integrating Against Noise

The Ito integral integrates a process $X_t$ with respect to Brownian motion. The key idea is that you sample $X_t$ at the left endpoint of each interval, which makes the integral a martingale.

$$\int_{0}^{T} X_t \, dW_t = \lim_{n\to\infty} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} X_{t_k} (W_{t_{k+1}} - W_{t_k})$$

Mean and variance

The Ito integral has mean 0, and its variance is the expected integral of $X_t^2$.

$$\mathbb{E}\left[\int_{0}^{T} X_t \, dW_t\right] = 0$$

Ito isometry

The Ito isometry gives a clean way to compute the second moment of the integral.

$$\mathbb{E}\left[\left(\int_{0}^{T} X_t \, dW_t\right)^2\right] = \mathbb{E}\left[\int_{0}^{T} X_t^2 \, dt\right]$$

Why left endpoints

Left-endpoint sampling prevents future information from leaking into the integral, keeping it adapted.

3) Ito's Lemma and Stochastic Differential Equations

Ito's lemma is the stochastic version of the chain rule. It powers models like the geometric Brownian motion used in finance.

$$dX_t = \mu(X_t, t) \, dt + \sigma(X_t, t) \, dW_t$$

Ito's lemma

For $Y_t = f(t, X_t)$, the differential includes an extra second-derivative term:

$$dY_t = \left(f_t + \mu f_x + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 f_{xx}\right)dt + \sigma f_x \, dW_t$$

Geometric Brownian motion

$$dS_t = \mu S_t \, dt + \sigma S_t \, dW_t$$ models random growth with drift and volatility.
