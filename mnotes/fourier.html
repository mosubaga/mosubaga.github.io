1) Fundamentals: Fourier Series & Transform

Any repeating signal can be rebuilt by stacking sine and cosine waves. A Fourier series does this for periodic signals, while a Fourier transform extends the idea to non-repeating signals by turning time into frequency.

$$f(t) = a_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left(a_n\cos(2\pi n t) + b_n\sin(2\pi n t)\right)$$
Fourier series Describes periodic signals as a sum of harmonics. Each harmonic has an amplitude and phase, letting you reconstruct the original loop perfectly if you include enough terms.
Fourier transform Converts any signal into a continuous spectrum of frequencies. The result tells you how much of each frequency is present and with what phase.
Fundamental frequency The lowest frequency in the series. It sets the overall pitch or rhythm, while higher frequencies add detail and texture.
Spectrum The frequency "fingerprint" of a signal. Peaks show dominant tones, while a wide spread indicates noise or sharp changes.
$$\mathcal{F}\{f(t)\}=F(\omega)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(t)e^{-i\omega t}\,dt$$

3) Fourier in Machine Learning

Fourier ideas show up inside ML models whenever we want to understand patterns across scales. We can transform inputs into frequency space to spot repeating structures, or use Fourier features to help neural networks learn high-frequency details.

Fourier features

By encoding inputs with sinusoidal features, models learn sharp edges and fine details faster, which is useful in vision, graphics, and implicit neural representations.

Spectral filtering

Transforming data into the frequency domain lets models remove noise or emphasize patterns at specific scales, improving robustness.

Convolutions

Convolution in time becomes multiplication in frequency. This makes large convolutions faster and easier to analyze.
