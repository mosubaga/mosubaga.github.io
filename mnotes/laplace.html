Turn Differential Equations into Algebra

1) Basics: What the Laplace Transform Does

For a time-domain signal $f(t)$, the Laplace transform builds a new function $F(s)$ that captures how much of each exponential $e^{-st}$ is present. The complex variable $s = \sigma + i\omega$ controls growth or decay plus oscillation.

$$\mathcal{L}\{f(t)\} = F(s) = \int_{0}^{\infty} f(t) e^{-st} \, dt$$
Linearity $\mathcal{L}\{af(t)+bg(t)\} = aF(s) + bG(s)$ lets you transform complicated mixtures term by term.
Derivatives become algebra $\mathcal{L}\{f'(t)\} = sF(s) - f(0)$ turns differential equations into algebraic ones.
Shifts and delays $\mathcal{L}\{f(t-a)u(t-a)\} = e^{-as}F(s)$ captures delayed inputs with a simple exponential factor.
Region of convergence The transform only exists when $f(t)e^{-\sigma t}$ is integrable, giving a band of valid $s$ values.

2) Inverse Transform: Coming Back Home

The inverse Laplace transform takes $F(s)$ and returns the original $f(t)$. In practice, engineers use tables, partial fractions, and convolution to invert transforms quickly.

$$f(t) = \mathcal{L}^{-1}\{F(s)\}$$

Common pairs

$\mathcal{L}\{1\}=\frac{1}{s}$, $\mathcal{L}\{e^{at}\}=\frac{1}{s-a}$, and $\mathcal{L}\{\sin bt\}=\frac{b}{s^2+b^2}$ are the workhorses of most solutions.

Partial fractions

When $F(s)$ is rational, split it into simpler pieces. Each term matches a table entry, so you can read off $f(t)$.

Convolution

Multiplication in the $s$-domain becomes convolution in time, which helps with systems and filters.

3) Applications: Why It Matters

Laplace transforms are the go-to tool for modeling physical systems with inputs, outputs, and initial conditions. They translate differential equations into transfer functions.

Control systems

Transfer functions like $G(s)=\frac{1}{s^2+2s+1}$ reveal stability and response speed.

Electrical circuits

Inductors and capacitors become algebraic impedances, so circuit equations simplify drastically.
