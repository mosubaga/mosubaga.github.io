Lagrange Multipliers in Soft Focus

Optimize with Constraints, Not Guesswork

Lagrange multipliers let you maximize or minimize a function while staying on a constraint. They turn a hard constrained problem into a clean system of equations.

1) The Basic Idea

Suppose you want to optimize $f(x, y)$ subject to a constraint $g(x, y) = c$. At the optimum, the gradient of $f$ points in the same direction as the gradient of $g$, because the constraint curve is tangent to the level set of $f$.

$$\nabla f(x, y) = \lambda \nabla g(x, y)$$
Constraint surface The set of all points that satisfy $g(x, y) = c$.
Multiplier $\lambda$ A scaling factor that balances the two gradients.
Geometric meaning The best point occurs when the contour of $f$ just touches the constraint.
System to solve Solve $\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g$ plus the constraint equation.

2) Step-by-Step Workflow

Step 1: Write the constraint

Express it as $g(x, y) = c$ (or $g(x, y, z) = c$ in 3D).

Step 2: Set up the Lagrangian

$\mathcal{L}(x, y, \lambda) = f(x, y) - \lambda(g(x, y) - c)$.

Step 3: Differentiate

Set $\nabla_x \mathcal{L} = 0$ and include the constraint.

Step 4: Solve and compare

Evaluate $f$ at each candidate to find max/min.

$$\mathcal{L}(x, y, \lambda) = f(x, y) - \lambda(g(x, y) - c)$$

3) Applications

Lagrange multipliers show up wherever a system has limited resources. In engineering, you maximize performance subject to safety or budget constraints. In economics, you maximize utility subject to a spending limit.

Economics

Maximize utility $U(x, y)$ given a budget $p_x x + p_y y = B$.
