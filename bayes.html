Bayes' Theorem

Bayes' theorem is a mathematical formula used to determine the probability of an event based on prior knowledge of conditions that might be related to that event. At its core, it is a tool for updating beliefs when new evidence is introduced. Rather than letting evidence determine a belief in a vacuum, the theorem treats it as a way to revise what you already knew.

How the Formula Works

The theorem is mathematically stated as:

\[ P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A)P(A)}{P(B)} \]

  • \(P(A)\) (The Prior): Your initial degree of belief in a hypothesis \(A\) before seeing any evidence.
  • \(P(B|A)\) (The Likelihood): The probability that the evidence \(B\) would occur if your hypothesis is true.
  • \(P(B)\) (The Evidence): The total probability of the evidence occurring under all possible scenarios.
  • \(P(A|B)\) (The Posterior): Your updated belief in the hypothesis after accounting for the new evidence.

Application 1: Medical Testing

One of the most famous applications is interpreting medical test results. A common mistake is assuming a test's accuracy, for example \(90\%\), is the same as the probability that a patient has the disease.

The Example

Imagine a screening for a disease that affects \(1\%\) of the population, the prior.

  • Sensitivity: The test correctly identifies \(80\%\) of people who have the disease.
  • Specificity: The test correctly identifies \(90.4\%\) of people who are healthy, meaning it has a \(9.6\%\) false positive rate.

If a patient tests positive, Bayes' theorem reveals the actual chance they have the disease is only about \(7.8\%\).

Why This Happens

Because the disease is rare, the number of false positives from the healthy \(99\%\) of the population overwhelms the true positives from the sick \(1\%\). To make this intuitive, consider \(1{,}000\) people:

  1. 10 people have the disease, and 8 will test positive.
  2. 990 people are healthy, and roughly 95 will test positive anyway.
  3. That makes 103 positive tests in total, and only 8 of those 103 actually have the disease, which is about \(7.8\%\).

Application 2: Detecting Spam and Fake Ads

Bayesian filtering is a cornerstone of modern email and internet safety. It treats the content of a message or ad as a series of tests.

  • The Event: Is this message spam?
  • The Test: Does it contain specific words such as "viagra," "free," or "winner"?

The Process

  1. Prior Probability: The filter starts with a general probability that any incoming message might be spam.
  2. Likelihood: The system is trained on many emails to learn that certain words have a much higher probability of appearing in spam than in normal messages.
  3. Updating: As the filter scans a message and finds trigger words, it uses Bayes' theorem to incrementally update the probability that the message is spam.
  4. Threshold: Rather than a simple yes/no blacklist, the filter calculates a final probability and compares it against a threshold.

Why Bayes' Theorem Matters

The most important lesson of Bayes' theorem is that new evidence should not replace your prior beliefs, but update them. If the prior is extremely low, such as a very rare disease or a very trusted sender, it takes a large amount of evidence, or a very high Bayes factor, to significantly change the final probability.

Odds Form and Bayes Factor

A very useful way to interpret Bayes' theorem is through odds. In odds form, the posterior odds equal the prior odds times the Bayes factor:

\[ \frac{P(A|B)}{P(A^c|B)} = \frac{P(A)}{P(A^c)} \cdot \frac{P(B|A)}{P(B|A^c)} \]

The ratio \( \frac{P(B|A)}{P(B|A^c)} \) is called the Bayes factor. It measures how strongly the evidence favors one hypothesis over another. This point of view is especially useful in statistics, scientific inference, and machine learning, because it separates what you believed before seeing the data from what the new data is telling you.

Naive Bayes in Machine Learning

One of the most practical descendants of Bayes' theorem is the Naive Bayes classifier. It assumes that features are conditionally independent given the class, which is often not exactly true but works surprisingly well in practice.

For features \(x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n\) and class \(C\), the classifier uses

\[ P(C \mid x_1, \dots, x_n) \propto P(C)\prod_{i=1}^{n} P(x_i \mid C) \]

This idea powers many simple but effective systems for text classification, spam detection, and document tagging.
