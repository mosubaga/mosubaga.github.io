Quantum Mechanics Notes

These notes collect the original Schrödinger and Dirac equation overview, followed by two standard Schrödinger equation examples: the harmonic oscillator and the free particle. The equations below use MathJax delimiters so they render directly in the browser.

The Schrödinger Equation was published in 1926, forming the basis for Quantum Mechanics.

The initial form is:

\[ \hat{H}\Psi = E\Psi \]

\[ -\dfrac{\hbar^{2}}{2m}\nabla^{2} \Psi + U\Psi = E\Psi = i\hbar \dfrac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t} \]


Where the solution must satisfy the condition

\[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \! \Psi^*\Psi \, \mathrm{d}x = 1 \]

Derivation Sketch: From Classical Energy to Schrödinger

Start with the classical non-relativistic energy of a particle in a potential \[ E = \frac{p^{2}}{2m} + U(\mathbf{r},t). \] De Broglie and Planck relations suggest \[ E \to i\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t}, \qquad \mathbf{p} \to -i\hbar \nabla. \] Substituting these operators into the classical energy expression gives the time-dependent Schrödinger equation \[ i\hbar \frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t} = -\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2m}\nabla^{2}\Psi + U\Psi. \] For time-independent potentials, separation of variables yields the stationary form \[ \hat{H}\Psi = E\Psi. \]

Why This Leads to the Dirac Equation

The Schrödinger equation is not relativistic; it uses the non-relativistic energy-momentum relation. To be consistent with special relativity, start from \[ E^{2} = p^{2}c^{2} + m^{2}c^{4}. \] Replacing \(E\) and \( \mathbf{p} \) by operators leads to the second-order Klein-Gordon equation. Dirac sought a first-order equation in both time and space: \[ i\hbar \frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t} = \left( c\,\boldsymbol{\alpha}\cdot \mathbf{p} + \beta m c^{2} \right)\Psi. \] Requiring that squaring this equation reproduces \[ E^{2} = p^{2}c^{2} + m^{2}c^{4} \] forces the matrices \(\boldsymbol{\alpha}\) and \(\beta\) to satisfy specific anticommutation relations. This introduces spin naturally and explains why the Dirac equation supersedes Schrödinger in the relativistic regime.

Solving the Harmonic Oscillator

To solve the Schrödinger equation for the harmonic oscillator, physicists generally use one of two primary methods: a "diabolically clever" algebraic technique using ladder operators or a "brute force" analytic approach using power series.

1. Setting up the Problem

The potential energy for a simple harmonic oscillator is given by \(V(x) = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2x^2\), where \(m\) is the mass and \(\omega\) is the classical frequency of oscillation. The time-independent Schrödinger equation for this system is:

\[ -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2x^2\psi = E\psi \]

2. The Algebraic Method (Ladder Operators)

This method factors the Hamiltonian using two non-Hermitian operators, \(a_-\) (the lowering or annihilation operator) and \(a_+\) (the raising or creation operator).

  • Operator Definitions: The operators are defined such that they satisfy the commutation relation \( [a_-, a_+] = 1 \).
  • Rewriting the Hamiltonian: The Hamiltonian can be expressed as \( H = \hbar\omega(a_+ a_- + 1/2) \).
  • The Ground State: The lowest energy state, \(\psi_0\), must be annihilated by the lowering operator, \( a_-\psi_0 = 0 \), because there is no lower state. Solving this first-order differential equation yields the Gaussian ground state:

\[ \psi_0(x) = \left(\frac{m\omega}{\pi\hbar}\right)^{1/4} e^{-\frac{m\omega}{2\hbar}x^2} \]

  • Generating Excited States: Higher states are generated by repeatedly applying the raising operator to the ground state: \( \psi_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n!}}(a_+)^n \psi_0 \).

3. The Analytic Method (Power Series)

This method solves the differential equation directly by introducing a dimensionless variable \( \xi = \sqrt{\frac{m\omega}{\hbar}}x \).

  • Peeling off the Exponential: To find a normalizable solution, one peels off the required Gaussian behavior at large distances: \( \psi(\xi) = h(\xi)e^{-\xi^2/2} \).
  • Hermite Polynomials: Substituting this into the Schrödinger equation leads to a differential equation for \(h(\xi)\), which is solved using a power series.
  • Quantization Condition: For the series to be normalizable, meaning it does not blow up at infinity, it must terminate into a polynomial. This requirement forces the energy to take only specific, discrete values.

4. The Final Results

Regardless of the method used, the solutions are consistent:

  • Energy Eigenvalues: The allowed energy levels are quantized and equally spaced:

\[ E_n = \left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right)\hbar\omega, \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \dots \]

  • Zero-Point Energy: Even in the ground state (\(n=0\)), the system has a non-zero energy of \(E_0 = \frac{1}{2}\hbar\omega\), a consequence of the uncertainty principle.
  • Eigenfunctions: The wave functions for the \(n\)-th state involve Hermite polynomials \(H_n\):

\[ \psi_n(x) = \left(\frac{m\omega}{\pi\hbar}\right)^{1/4} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n n!}} H_n(\xi) e^{-\xi^2/2} \]

  • Spatial Features: Higher-energy wave functions oscillate more rapidly and are more spread out into classically forbidden regions where \(E < V\).

Solving the Free Particle

Solving the Schrödinger equation for a free particle, where the potential energy is \(V(x) = 0\) everywhere, is a surprisingly subtle process because the standard stationary-state solutions are not physically realizable on their own.

1. The Time-Independent Schrödinger Equation

For a free particle, the equation simplifies to:

\[ -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} = E\psi \quad \text{or} \quad \frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} = -k^2\psi \]

where the wave number is defined as \(k \equiv \frac{\sqrt{2mE}}{\hbar}\). Since there are no boundary conditions to restrict the possible values of \(k\), the free particle can carry any positive energy.

2. Separable Solutions (Stationary States)

The general solution is typically written in exponential form:

\[ \psi(x) = Ae^{ikx} + Be^{-ikx} \]

When the standard time dependence \(e^{-iEt/\hbar}\) is added, the full wave function becomes a combination of traveling waves:

\[ \Psi(x, t) = Ae^{ik(x - \frac{\hbar k}{2m}t)} + Be^{-ik(x + \frac{\hbar k}{2m}t)} \]

  • Terms with \(e^{ikx}\) represent waves traveling to the right.
  • Terms with \(e^{-ikx}\) represent waves traveling to the left.
  • These waves carry a definite momentum \(p = \hbar k\).

3. The Normalization Problem

The primary difficulty is that these stationary states are not normalizable. The integral of \(|\Psi|^2\) over all space is infinite, meaning there is no such thing as a free particle with a perfectly definite energy and momentum. Such a state would have a completely undetermined position according to the uncertainty principle.

4. The Physical Solution: Wave Packets

To represent a real particle, one must use a wave packet, a linear combination or superposition of these stationary states. The general solution takes the form:

\[ \Psi(x, t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \phi(k) e^{i(kx - \frac{\hbar k^2}{2m}t)} \, dk \]

The function \(\phi(k)\) determines the shape of the packet and is found using the initial state \(\Psi(x, 0)\) through a Fourier transform:

\[ \phi(k) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \Psi(x, 0) e^{-ikx} \, dx \]

5. Key Characteristics of the Motion

  • Group vs. Phase Velocity: Individual ripples in the wave move at the phase velocity \(v_p = \frac{\hbar k}{2m}\), but the overall envelope of the packet moves at the group velocity \(v_g = \frac{\hbar k}{m}\). Only the group velocity matches the classical speed of the particle.
  • Spreading: Unlike classical particles, quantum wave packets naturally spread out and flatten over time, meaning the particle's position becomes increasingly uncertain as it moves.
  • Conservation: While the packet spreads in position space, the probability distribution of momentum, \( |\phi(k)|^2 \), remains constant over time, reflecting the conservation of momentum.
