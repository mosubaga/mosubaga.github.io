Zombies vs Humans


Given the following info in a town where a virus spreads that turns humans into zombies.

  • Every hour, humans turns into 20% of humans to zombies.
  • Luckily, an antidote exists and can fix zombies to humans but at 10% an hour.

What is going to happen eventually?


We can write the above in a following equation:

\[\left\{ \begin{array}{ll} h_t = 0.8h_{t-1} + 0.1z_{t-1}\\ z_t = 0.2h_{t-1} + 0.9z_{t-1}\\ \end{array}\right.\]

Can be organized as:

\[\begin{bmatrix} 0.8 & 0.1\\ 0.2 & 0.9 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} h_{t-1}\\ z_{t-1} \end{bmatrix}= \begin{bmatrix} h_{t}\\ z_{t} \end{bmatrix}\]

The matrix is a Markov matrix and the eigenvalues are:

\[ \lambda = 0.7, 1 \]

Can calculate the eigenvectors as eigenvalues are determined.

\[ v_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 0.5\\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \space v_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ -1 \end{bmatrix} \]

From the findings above, if there are 1500 people in town and if human = 500 and zombies = 1000:

\[\begin{bmatrix} 0.8 & 0.1\\ 0.2 & 0.9 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 500\\ 1000 \end{bmatrix}= \begin{bmatrix} 500\\1000 \end{bmatrix}\]

No matter how much progresses, the number of humans and zombies will remain the same.

Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

For a square matrix \(A\), a nonzero vector \(v\) is an eigenvector and \(\lambda\) is its eigenvalue if

\[ A v = \lambda v. \]

Geometrically, \(A\) acts on \(v\) by stretching (and possibly flipping) without changing its direction. To find eigenvalues, solve the characteristic equation:

\[ \det(A - \lambda I) = 0. \]

For each root \(\lambda\), solve \((A - \lambda I)v = 0\) to get the corresponding eigenvectors (any nonzero vector in the null space).

Why they matter

  • They reveal invariant directions of a linear transformation.
  • They diagonalize matrices (when possible), simplifying powers \(A^k\) and exponentials \(e^{tA}\).
  • They encode long-term behavior in dynamical systems, Markov chains, ODEs, and PCA.

Quick example (2Ã—2)

Let

\[ A=\begin{bmatrix} a & b\\ c & d \end{bmatrix}. \]

Then

\[ \det(A-\lambda I)= \begin{vmatrix} a-\lambda & b\\ c & d-\lambda \end{vmatrix} = (a-\lambda)(d-\lambda)-bc = \lambda^2-(a+d)\lambda+(ad-bc). \]

Solve the quadratic for \(\lambda\); for each \(\lambda\), solve \((A-\lambda I)v=0\) to get an eigenvector.

Markov Matrices (Stochastic Matrices)

A Markov transition matrix encodes probabilities of moving between states. In the column-stochastic convention (used in the example below):

  • All entries are nonnegative.
  • Each column sums to 1 (total probability is preserved).

If \(x_t\) is a probability vector (entries nonnegative, summing to 1), the evolution is

\[ x_{t} = A\,x_{t-1}. \]

Key facts:

  • \(\lambda=1\) is always an eigenvalue of a stochastic matrix.
  • An eigenvector for \(\lambda=1\) (normalized to sum to 1) is a stationary distribution \(x_*\) with \(A x_* = x_*\).
  • If all other eigenvalues satisfy \(|\lambda|<1\) and the chain is irreducible and aperiodic, then \(x_t \to x_*\) as \(t\to\infty\).

Connecting to the Zombies vs Humans example

With

\[ A=\begin{bmatrix} 0.8 & 0.1\\ 0.2 & 0.9 \end{bmatrix}, \]

the eigenvalues are \(\lambda_1=1\) and \(\lambda_2=0.7\). A stationary vector (eigenvector for \(\lambda=1\)) is proportional to

\[ v_1=\begin{bmatrix} 0.5\\ 1 \end{bmatrix}, \]

which normalizes to the stationary distribution

\[ x_*=\frac{1}{1.5}\begin{bmatrix} 0.5\\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \tfrac{1}{3}\\ \tfrac{2}{3}\end{bmatrix}. \]

Since the second eigenvalue has magnitude \(0.7<1\), any initial state converges to \(x_*\). For a population of 1500, that's about 500 humans and 1000 zombies in the long run.
