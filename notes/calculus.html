Calculus grew out of the 17th-century work of Newton and Leibniz on motion and change, building on earlier ideas from Archimedes and others. Euler's Identity

Euler’s identity is the elegant special case of Euler’s formula:

\[ e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0 \]

It links five fundamental constants—e, i, π, 1, and 0—in a single, compact equation. More generally, Euler’s formula states

\[ e^{i\theta} = \cos \theta + i \sin \theta. \]

Quick proof sketch (via power series)

Start with the Taylor series (valid for all real or complex x):

\[ e^x = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{x^n}{n!},\quad \cos x = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(-1)^n\frac{x^{2n}}{(2n)!},\quad \sin x = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(-1)^n\frac{x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}. \]

Substitute x = iθ into e^x:

\[ e^{i\theta} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(i\theta)^n}{n!} = \left(\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(-1)^n\frac{\theta^{2n}}{(2n)!}\right) + i\left(\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(-1)^n\frac{\theta^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}\right) = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta. \]

Setting \(\theta=\pi\) gives \(e^{i\pi}=\cos\pi+i\sin\pi=-1\), hence \(e^{i\pi}+1=0\).

A bit of history

Leonhard Euler (1707–1783) developed the complex exponential and its link to trigonometry in the 1740s–1748, showing that exponential growth and rotation on the complex plane are two views of the same object. The special case \(e^{i\pi}+1=0\) later became celebrated as "Euler’s identity" for its surprising simplicity and depth.

Where it’s used

  • Fourier analysis: Express sinusoids as complex exponentials to simplify transforms, filtering, and signal synthesis: \(\cos\theta=\tfrac{e^{i\theta}+e^{-i\theta}}{2}\), \(\sin\theta=\tfrac{e^{i\theta}-e^{-i\theta}}{2i}\).
  • Differential equations: Solve linear ODEs with oscillatory solutions via \(e^{i\omega t}\), then take real parts.
  • Phasors in circuits: Represent AC signals and impedances as complex numbers; rotation by phase is multiplication by \(e^{i\phi}\).
  • Rotations in the plane: Multiplying by \(e^{i\theta}\) rotates a complex number by \(\theta\), useful in geometry and graphics.
  • Quantum mechanics: Wavefunctions and time evolution use complex exponentials, e.g., \(e^{-iEt/\hbar}\).
