Probability is a branch in mathematics on how likely an event is to occur. Most probalilities are expressed as fractions, decimal numbers or percentages and are measured on a scaled between zero and one. If an event is impossible to occur, the probability is 0. A certain event has a probability of 1. Calculating permutations and combinations are essential on finding probabilities of an event.
To calculate permutation:

\[ _{n}P_{r} = \dfrac{n!}{(n - r)!} \]

To calculate combination:

\[ _{n}C_{r} = \dfrac{n!}{r!(n - r)!} \]


In probability theory, conditional probability is a measure of the probability of an event occurring, given that another event (by assumption, presumption, assertion or evidence) is already known to have occurred. This particular method relies on event A occurring with some sort of relationship with another event B. This can be written by :
\[ P(A \lvert B) = \frac{P(B \lvert A)P(A)}{P(B)} \]

Monty Hall Problem — Bayes' Theorem Explanation

The Monty Hall problem is a probability puzzle based on a game show scenario with three doors. You pick one door; the host, who knows what's behind each door, opens another door revealing a goat; then you are offered the choice to stay with your original door or switch to the remaining closed door.

Summary answer


If you switch after the host opens a goat door, your probability of winning the car is 2/3.
If you stay with your original choice, your probability is 1/3.

Formal derivation with Bayes' theorem

Notation (label the doors 1, 2, 3). Suppose you initially choose door 1. Define events:

  • A1 = "the car is behind door 1"
  • A2 = "the car is behind door 2"
  • A3 = "the car is behind door 3"
  • B = "the host opens door 3" (revealing a goat)

We want the posterior probabilities P(Ai | B). Bayes' theorem says

P(A_i | B) = P(B | A_i) * P(A_i) / P(B)

Start with the priors (before the host opens any door):

P(A1) = P(A2) = P(A3) = 1/3

Compute the likelihoods P(B | Ai) — the chance the host opens door 3 given where the car actually is:

  1. If the car is behind door 1 (A1): then the host must open either door 2 or door 3. If the host chooses uniformly at random between the two goat doors, the probability he opens door 3 is 1/2. So P(B | A1) = 1/2.
  2. If the car is behind door 2 (A2): the host cannot open door 2 (it has the car) and must open door 3 (the only goat door left). So P(B | A2) = 1.
  3. If the car is behind door 3 (A3): the host will not open door 3 because it hides the car. So P(B | A3) = 0.

Compute the total probability P(B) by the law of total probability:

P(B) = Σ_i P(B | A_i) P(A_i) = (1/2)*(1/3) + 1*(1/3) + 0*(1/3) = 1/6 + 1/3 = 1/2

Now apply Bayes' theorem to get the posteriors.

P(A1 | B) = (P(B | A1) * P(A1)) / P(B) = (1/2 * 1/3) / (1/2) = (1/6) / (1/2) = 1/3
P(A2 | B) = (P(B | A2) * P(A2)) / P(B) = (1 * 1/3) / (1/2) = (1/3) / (1/2) = 2/3
P(A3 | B) = 0

Interpretation: after the host opens door 3, the probability the car is behind your original choice (door 1) is still 1/3, and the probability it is behind the other unopened door (door 2) is 2/3. So switching doubles your chance of winning.

Short intuitive explanation

When you first pick a door you have a 1/3 chance of being right and a 2/3 chance of being wrong. The host's action of opening a goat door does not change the initial 1/3 chance your chosen door had — instead it concentrates the original 2/3 probability of "car is not behind your door" onto the single unopened door the host left. Thus switching wins with probability 2/3.

Generalization to n doors

With n doors, you pick one (probability 1/n of being correct). The host — who knows where the prize is — opens n−2 goat doors, leaving one other closed door. If the host always avoids the prize and never opens your picked door, switching to the single remaining closed door yields probability (n−1)/n of winning (because the initial 1/n chance you picked correctly remains with your original door, and the complementary (n−1)/n probability transfers to the other unopened door).
