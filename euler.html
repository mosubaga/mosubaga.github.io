Derivation of the Euler–Lagrange Equation

1. Brief historical context and motivation

Newton's formulation of classical mechanics (late 17th century) describes motion via forces and second-order differential equations, for example \(m\ddot{q}=F\), typically written in Cartesian coordinates. While extremely successful, the Newtonian approach can become cumbersome for constrained systems (such as pendulums, rolling motion, or motion on curved surfaces) and for problems where symmetry and choice of generalized coordinates are essential.

In the 18th century, Leonhard Euler and Joseph-Louis Lagrange helped develop the calculus of variations and showed how many mechanical problems can be reformulated as an extremum principle. In mechanics this takes the form of Hamilton's principle: the true path \(q(t)\) taken by a system between fixed endpoints makes the action

\begin{equation} S[q] = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} L\bigl(q(t), \dot{q}(t), t\bigr)\,dt \end{equation}

stationary. Here \(q\) denotes generalized coordinates (not necessarily Cartesian), and \(L=T-V\) is the Lagrangian, the difference between kinetic and potential energy for many common systems. This variational viewpoint turns constraints and coordinate changes into systematic procedures and exposes conserved quantities through symmetries (as later formalized by Noether's theorem).

2. Lagrangian vs. Hamiltonian (Legendre transform)

Given a Lagrangian \(L(q,\dot{q},t)\), define the conjugate momentum

\begin{equation} p := \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}}. \end{equation}

When the map \(\dot{q}\mapsto p\) can be inverted (non-degeneracy), one defines the Hamiltonian as the Legendre transform of \(L\) in the velocity variable:

\begin{equation} H(q,p,t) := p\,\dot{q} - L(q,\dot{q},t), \end{equation}

where \(\dot{q}\) on the right-hand side is understood to be expressed in terms of \((q,p,t)\). The Hamiltonian formulation rewrites the dynamics as a first-order system (Hamilton's equations)

\begin{equation} \dot{q} = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p}, \qquad \dot{p} = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q}. \end{equation}

For many mechanical systems with \(L=T-V\) and no explicit time dependence, \(H\) coincides with the total energy \(T+V\), and the Hamiltonian picture is especially useful in canonical transformations and as a bridge to quantum mechanics.

3. The variational problem

Let \(x\) be a real variable and let \(y=y(x)\) be a differentiable function on an interval \([a,b]\). Consider the functional

\begin{equation} J[y] \;=\; \int_a^b L\bigl(x, y(x), y'(x)\bigr)\, dx, \end{equation}

where \(L(x,y,p)\) is a given function (the Lagrangian), assumed to be continuously differentiable in its arguments.

We seek a function \(y\) that makes \(J[y]\) stationary (i.e. extremizes \(J\)) among all admissible functions satisfying fixed endpoint conditions

\begin{equation} y(a)=y_a, \qquad y(b)=y_b. \end{equation}

4. Variations and the first variation

Take an arbitrary variation \(\eta(x)\) such that

\begin{equation} \eta(a)=\eta(b)=0, \end{equation}

and consider a one-parameter family of perturbed functions

\begin{equation} y_\varepsilon(x) = y(x) + \varepsilon \eta(x), \end{equation}

where \(\varepsilon\) is a real parameter. Define

\begin{equation} \Phi(\varepsilon) := J[y_\varepsilon] = \int_a^b L\bigl(x, y(x)+\varepsilon\eta(x), y'(x)+\varepsilon\eta'(x)\bigr)\, dx. \end{equation}

If \(y\) is a stationary point of \(J\), then \(\Phi'(0)=0\). Differentiate under the integral sign:

\begin{align} \Phi'(0) &= \int_a^b \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial y}\eta + \frac{\partial L}{\partial y'}\eta' \right)dx. \end{align}

This quantity is called the first variation and is often denoted \(\delta J\).

5. Integration by parts

We integrate the term involving \(\eta'\) by parts:

\begin{align} \int_a^b \frac{\partial L}{\partial y'}\,\eta'\,dx &= \left[\frac{\partial L}{\partial y'}\,\eta\right]_{a}^{b} - \int_a^b \frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial y'}\right)\eta \,dx. \end{align}

The boundary term vanishes because \(\eta(a)=\eta(b)=0\). Therefore,

\begin{equation} \Phi'(0) = \int_a^b \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial y} - \frac{d}{dx}\frac{\partial L}{\partial y'} \right)\eta \,dx. \end{equation}

6. The fundamental lemma and the Euler–Lagrange equation

Since \(\eta(x)\) is arbitrary except for vanishing at the endpoints, the fundamental lemma of the calculus of variations implies that the coefficient of \(\eta\) must vanish for all \(x\in(a,b)\):

\begin{equation} \frac{\partial L}{\partial y}(x,y,y') \;-\; \frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial y'}(x,y,y')\right) \;=\; 0. \end{equation}

This is the Euler–Lagrange equation.

7. Example

Let

\begin{equation} L(x,y,y') = \frac{1}{2}(y')^2 - V(y), \end{equation}

where \(V(y)\) is a differentiable potential. Then

\begin{equation} \frac{\partial L}{\partial y} = -V'(y), \qquad \frac{\partial L}{\partial y'} = y'. \end{equation}

The Euler–Lagrange equation becomes

\begin{equation} -V'(y) - \frac{d}{dx}(y') = 0 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad y'' + V'(y) = 0, \end{equation}

which resembles Newton's second law in one dimension.
