Old & Modern Metrics

From batting average to wRC+ — every formula, explained.

Hitting metrics have evolved from simple counting stats to context-adjusted, run-value-weighted measures that better isolate a batter's true contribution regardless of ballpark or era.

Batting Average BA / AVG
Traditional

The oldest and most cited hitting stat.

Formula
BA= HAB

H = Hits  ·  AB = At-Bats
Measures how often a batter gets a hit per official at-bat. Walks, HBP, and sacrifice flies are excluded — a key weakness.

Poor <.230 Avg .260 Good .280 Elite .300+
Slugging Percentage SLG
Traditional

Measures raw power output per at-bat.

Formula
SLG= 1B+2⋅2B +3⋅3B +4⋅HR AB

Weights hits by bases earned. A home run counts four times more than a single. Ignores walks entirely — missing a quarter of all plate appearances.

Poor <.350 Avg .420 Good .480 Elite .550+
Runs Batted In RBI
Traditional

Credited when a batter's plate appearance scores a run.

Context
RBI=Counting Stat

No formula — a raw count. Heavily dependent on lineup position and teammates on base. A cleanup hitter on a great team will always accumulate more RBI than an equally skilled leadoff hitter.

Avg starter 60–75 Good 90+ Elite 120+
On-Base Plus Slugging OPS
Bridge

The gateway from traditional to modern analysis.

Formula
OPS=OBP+SLG

Simple sum of On-Base and Slugging percentages. Correlates well with run scoring. Limitation: OBP and SLG are not on the same scale — adding them is mathematically imprecise but practically useful.

Poor <.650 Avg .720 Good .800 Elite .900+
On-Base Percentage OBP
Modern

"Get on base" — the single most important offensive skill.

Formula
OBP= H+BB+HBP AB+BB+HBP+SF

HBP = Hit by Pitch  ·  SF = Sacrifice Fly
Includes walks and HBP that BA ignores. A batter who walks 100 times is just as valuable as one who gets 100 extra singles — OBP captures both.

Poor <.300 Avg .320 Good .360 Elite .400+
Isolated Power ISO
Modern

Pure extra-base power, stripped of singles.

Formula
ISO=SLG−BA= 2B+ 2⋅3B+ 3⋅HR AB

Removes singles from slugging. Isolates the player's ability to hit for extra bases regardless of contact rate. A pure power metric with no contact noise.

Poor <.100 Avg .140 Good .200 Elite .250+
Batting Avg on Balls in Play BABIP
Modern

Separates skill from luck on contact.

Formula
BABIP= H−HR AB−SO−HR+SF

Excludes home runs and strikeouts (outcomes independent of fielding). League average ≈ .300. A batter at .380 BABIP is likely lucky; one at .220 is likely unlucky — regression is expected.

Unlucky <.270 League avg .300 Skill or luck? .350+
OPS+ OPS+
Modern

OPS adjusted for ballpark and league — era-neutral.

Formula
OPS+ =100⋅ OBPOBPlg + SLGSLGlg −1

100 = exactly league average. Above 100 = better than average. Park-factor adjusted. Enables direct cross-era comparison: Babe Ruth's 206 vs Mike Trout's 173 are apples-to-apples.

Avg 100 Good 120+ Star 140+ MVP 160+
Weighted On-Base Average wOBA
Modern

Assigns run values to each offensive outcome.

Formula — 2024 weights
0.69⋅BB +0.72⋅HBP +0.89⋅1B +1.27⋅2B +1.62⋅3B +2.10⋅HR AB+BB−IBB +SF+HBP

Weights derived from run expectancy matrices. A HR (2.10) is worth ~3× a walk (0.69). The gold standard for measuring offensive value per plate appearance.

Poor <.290 Avg .320 Good .360 Elite .400+
Weighted Runs Created Plus wRC+
Modern

The most complete, park-adjusted offensive metric.

Formula
wRC+ =100⋅ wOBA−wOBAlg wOBAscale + RlgPA RlgPA

Adjusts wOBA for park factors and league run environment. 100 = league average. The single best number for "who is the best hitter?" across any era or ballpark.

Avg 100 Good 120+ Star 140+ MVP 160+
Strikeout & Walk Rate K% / BB%
Modern

Plate discipline — what happens before the ball is put in play.

Formulas
K%=SOPA BB%=BBPA

PA = Plate Appearances (AB + BB + HBP + SH + SF)
K% over 30% signals strikeout liability. BB% over 12% is elite discipline. The spread K%−BB% is a quick single-number plate-discipline summary.

Avg K% 22% Elite BB% 12%+ Good spread BB%−K% >−5%

Pitching metrics evolved to separate what a pitcher controls (strikeouts, walks, home runs) from what the defense behind him controls (balls in play). The modern goal: measure true pitching skill, not luck.
Traditional Old-school stat
Modern Sabermetric
Earned Run Average ERA
Traditional

Runs allowed per 9 innings — the oldest pitching measure.

Formula
ERA= ER⋅9IP

ER = Earned Runs  ·  IP = Innings Pitched
Excludes unearned runs (errors). Still affected by the quality of defense behind the pitcher and the dimensions of the ballpark.

Poor 5.00+ Avg 4.20 Good 3.50 Elite <3.00
Wins & Losses W / L
Traditional

The most misused stat in baseball history.

Win Condition (simplified)
W= Pitcher leads when team takes final lead

Awarded by official scorer judgment. A pitcher can throw 8 shutout innings and get a no-decision. Another can allow 5 runs and win because teammates scored 10. Wins measure teams, not pitchers.

Avg starter 10–12 W Ace season 18+ W
Strikeouts per 9 Innings K/9
Traditional

Raw strikeout rate — one of the few traditional stats still trusted.

Formula
K/9= SO⋅9IP

Strikeouts are the one outcome where the defense plays no role. Always a reliable dominance indicator. League average has risen from ~5.0 in the 1970s to ~8.5 today — era context matters.

Avg (modern) 8.5 Good 10+ Elite 12+
Winning Percentage W%
Traditional

Wins as a fraction of decisions.

Formula
W%= WW+L

Slightly better than raw wins since it accounts for losses. Still entirely team-dependent. A pitcher on a 100-win team will always show a higher W% than an equally skilled pitcher on a 70-win team.

Avg .500 Elite .650+
Walks + Hits per Inning WHIP
Modern

How many baserunners does the pitcher allow per inning?

Formula
WHIP= BB+HIP

A cleaner baserunner-prevention metric than ERA. Still affected by defense (hits can be errors) and doesn't weight outcomes by severity — a walk and a homer each count as 1.

Poor 1.50+ Avg 1.30 Good 1.15 Elite <1.00
Strikeout-to-Walk Ratio K/BB
Modern

Dominance and control in a single ratio.

Formula
K/BB= SOBB

Both SO and BB are defense-independent. A high K/BB means the pitcher misses bats AND throws strikes — the combination that defines aces. Curt Schilling's career K/BB of 4.38 is the highest among pitchers with 3,000+ IP.

Poor <1.5 Avg 2.0 Good 3.0 Elite 4.0+
Fielding Independent Pitching FIP
Modern

ERA calculated using only what the pitcher controls.

Formula
FIP= 13⋅HR +3⋅ BB+HBP −2⋅SO IP +CFIP

CFIP ≈ 3.10 (recalibrated each season to match league ERA). Ignores all balls in play. If ERA >> FIP, the defense was poor. If ERA << FIP, the pitcher was lucky. FIP predicts future ERA better than ERA itself.

Poor 5.00+ Avg 4.20 Good 3.50 Elite <3.00
Expected FIP xFIP
Modern

FIP with home-run luck removed — the purest pitching metric.

Formula
xFIP= 13⋅ FB⋅HR/FBlg +3⋅ BB+HBP −2⋅SO IP +CFIP

FB = Fly balls  ·  HR/FBlg = League avg HR-per-fly-ball (~10.5%)
Replaces actual HR with what league-average HR/FB rate would produce. Removes HR luck entirely. Best predictor of next-season ERA.

Avg ~4.20 Elite <3.20
ERA Minus ERA−
Modern

Park- and league-adjusted ERA — like OPS+ but for pitchers.

Formula
ERA− =100⋅ ERA ERAlg⋅PFpark

PFpark = Park Factor (Coors Field ≈ 1.15, Petco Park ≈ 0.92)
100 = league average. Lower is better. An ERA− of 75 means 25% fewer runs than a league-average pitcher in the same park. Enables direct cross-era comparison.

Avg 100 Good 85 Elite <70
Pitcher BABIP BABIPP
Modern

How much ball-in-play luck can a pitcher control?

Formula
BABIPP = H−HR BFP−SO−BB−HBP−HR

Research shows pitchers have very little control over BABIP — it mostly reflects fielding and luck. League average ≈ .300. This is why FIP outperforms ERA as a predictive tool.

Lucky <.270 League avg .300 Unlucky .340+
Pitcher K% & BB% K% / BB%
Modern

Rate stats that don't inflate with more innings pitched.

Formulas
K%=SOBFP BB%=BBBFP

BFP = Batters Faced by Pitcher. Preferred over K/9 and BB/9 because both use the same denominator — making K%−BB% a valid, single-number plate-discipline summary for the pitcher.

Avg K% 22% Elite K% 28%+ Elite BB% <6%
