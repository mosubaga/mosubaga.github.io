Brownian Motion and Itô's Lemma

Brownian motion (also known as the Wiener process) is a mathematical model used to describe random, continuous motion. It is one of the most important concepts in probability theory, with applications ranging from physics to finance.

1. The Origin and History

The process is named after the Scottish botanist Robert Brown, who in 1828 observed pollen grains suspended in water moving in a highly irregular, jittery fashion. This movement was later understood to be caused by the pollen grains being bombarded by water molecules from all directions. In the early 20th century, mathematicians like Norbert Wiener developed the formal mathematical framework for this motion, which is why it is often called the Wiener process.

2. Intuition: The Limit of a Random Walk

The easiest way to visualize Brownian motion is to imagine a random walk. Think of a person tossing a fair coin:

  • If it’s heads, they take a small step forward.
  • If it’s tails, they take a small step backward.

As the size of the steps gets smaller and the frequency of the steps gets faster, this jagged movement eventually smooths out into a continuous but highly "wiggly" path. This limiting process is exactly what Brownian motion is.

3. The Three Defining Rules

For a process to be considered a Standard Brownian Motion, it must follow three specific rules:

  1. Independent Increments: What happens in the future does not depend on the past. If you know where the process is now, knowing how it got there doesn't help you predict its next move.
  2. Stationary Increments: The "distance" the process moves over a specific time interval depends only on the length of that interval, not on when it occurs. For example, the movement between minute 1 and minute 2 has the same probability distribution as the movement between minute 10 and minute 11.
  3. Continuous Paths: The path never jumps. You can draw the entire history of a Brownian motion without ever lifting your pen from the paper.

Additionally, it always starts at zero (\(B_0 = 0\)).

4. The "Wiggly" Nature of the Path

While the path is continuous, it is also nowhere differentiable. This means that if you zoom in on a Brownian path, it remains just as jagged and rough no matter how far you zoom; it never "smooths out" into a straight line.

Another strange property is its infinite variation: in any finite interval of time, no matter how small, a Brownian particle actually travels an infinite total distance because it is constantly oscillating back and forth at an infinite frequency.

5. Key Statistical Concepts

  • Normal Distribution: The position of a Brownian particle at any time \(t\) follows a Normal (Gaussian) distribution. Its average (mean) position is 0, and its spread (variance) is equal to \(t\).
  • The Martingale Property: Brownian motion is a "fair game". If you are at a certain position now, your expected position at any point in the future is exactly where you are right now.
  • Reflection Principle: If a Brownian motion reaches a certain value \(a\), the probability that it will eventually end up above that value is the same as the probability it ends up below it.

6. Common Variants

  • Drift: You can add a constant "trend" to Brownian motion, making it generally move upward or downward over time.
  • Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM): This is a version where the percentage change is a Brownian motion rather than the absolute change. This is the standard model used to represent stock prices, because stock prices cannot be negative and investors tend to think in terms of percentage returns.
  • Brownian Bridge: This is a Brownian motion that is "pinned" so it starts at zero and is guaranteed to end at zero at a specific future time.

7. Why Does It Matter?

Brownian motion is the foundation of Stochastic Calculus. It provides a way to model "noise" in systems that were previously thought to be deterministic. It is also deeply connected to the Heat Equation in physics, as the way heat diffuses through a metal rod is mathematically identical to the way a collection of Brownian particles would spread out.

The relationship between Brownian motion and Itô's Lemma (also known as Itô's formula) stems from the unique path properties of Brownian motion, specifically its non-differentiability and non-zero quadratic variation.

1. The Failure of Classical Calculus

In ordinary calculus, the change in a smooth function \(f(t)\) is given by \(df = f'(t)dt\). This is possible because we can approximate the change using a first-order Taylor expansion, where higher-order terms like \((dt)^2\) are negligible and vanish in the limit.

However, Brownian motion (\(B_t\)) is nowhere differentiable and has infinite total variation. This means its paths are too "jagged" to be integrated using classical Riemann-Stieltjes theory. If we attempt a Taylor expansion on a function of Brownian motion, \(f(B_t)\), the second-order term involving \((\Delta B_t)^2\) does not vanish.

2. The Fundamental Rule: \((dB_t)^2 = dt\)

The core bridge between Brownian motion and Itô's Lemma is the behavior of its quadratic variation. For a standard Brownian motion, the square of its increments \((\Delta B_t)^2\) is approximately equal to the time increment \(\Delta t\). Formally, the quadratic variation converges to \(t\) as the partition size goes to zero:

\[ \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum (B_{t_{j+1}} - B_{t_j})^2 = t \text{ (in probability)} \]

In differential notation, this is expressed as the formal rule: \((dB_t)^2 = dt\).

3. Derivation via Taylor Expansion

To find the differential of a function \(f(B_t)\), we use a second-order Taylor expansion:

\[ f(B_{t+\Delta t}) - f(B_t) \approx f'(B_t)\Delta B_t + \frac{1}{2}f''(B_t)(\Delta B_t)^2 \]

In the limit as \(\Delta t \to 0\):

  • The first term \(f'(B_t)\Delta B_t\) becomes the stochastic integral \(\int f'(B_s)dB_s\).
  • Because \((\Delta B_t)^2 \approx \Delta t\), the second term \(\frac{1}{2}f''(B_t)(\Delta B_t)^2\) becomes \(\frac{1}{2}\int f''(B_s)ds\).

4. The Result: Itô's Lemma

This leads to the fundamental formula of stochastic calculus. For a function \(f \in C^2\):

\[ f(B_t) = f(B_0) + \int_{0}^{t} f'(B_s)dB_s + \frac{1}{2}\int_{0}^{t} f''(B_s)ds \]

Or in the more common differential form:

\[ df(B_t) = f'(B_t)dB_t + \frac{1}{2}f''(B_t)dt \]

The term \(\frac{1}{2}f''(B_t)dt\) is known as the Itô correction term. It represents the extra "drift" created by the volatility of Brownian motion, which is absent in deterministic calculus.

5. Extension to Itô Processes

Itô's Lemma also applies to more general Itô processes of the form \(dX_t = u_t dt + v_t dB_t\). For a function \(g(t, X_t)\), the differential is:

\[ dg(t, X_t) = \frac{\partial g}{\partial t}dt + \frac{\partial g}{\partial x}dX_t + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2 g}{\partial x^2}(dX_t)^2 \]

Using the formal multiplication rules (where \(dt \cdot dt = 0\) and \(dB_t \cdot dB_t = dt\)), the term \((dX_t)^2\) simplifies to \(v_t^2 dt\), allowing for the calculation of complex stochastic differentials.

Itô's Lemma (Derivation)

Setup

Let \( W_t \) be a standard Brownian motion, and let \( X_t \) satisfy the stochastic differential equation

\[ dX_t = a(t,X_t)\,dt + b(t,X_t)\,dW_t. \]

Let \( f(t,x) \in C^{1,2} \), meaning:

  • once continuously differentiable in \( t \)
  • twice continuously differentiable in \( x \)

We define:

\[ Y_t = f(t, X_t). \]

Key Idea: Second-Order Expansion

We consider a small increment:

\[ \Delta X \approx a(t,X_t)\Delta t + b(t,X_t)\Delta W. \]

Expand using Taylor:

\[ \Delta f = f_t \Delta t + f_x \Delta X + \frac12 f_{xx} (\Delta X)^2 + f_{tx}\Delta t\,\Delta X + \frac12 f_{tt}(\Delta t)^2. \]

Important Scaling

Brownian motion satisfies:

\[ \Delta W \sim (\Delta t)^{1/2} \]

Thus:

  • \( (\Delta W)^2 \sim \Delta t \) and is important.
  • \( \Delta t \Delta W \sim (\Delta t)^{3/2} \) is negligible.
  • \( (\Delta t)^2 \) is negligible.

Compute \((\Delta X)^2\)

\[ (\Delta X)^2 \approx b^2 (\Delta W)^2 \]

Thus:

\[ (\Delta X)^2 \leadsto b^2 \Delta t \]

Substitute Back

\[ \Delta f \approx f_t \Delta t + f_x(a\Delta t + b\Delta W) + \frac12 f_{xx} b^2 \Delta t \]

Group terms:

\[ \Delta f \approx \left(f_t + a f_x + \frac12 b^2 f_{xx}\right)\Delta t + b f_x \Delta W \]

Final Result (Itô’s Lemma)

\[ \boxed{ df(t,X_t) = \left( f_t(t,X_t) + a(t,X_t) f_x(t,X_t) + \frac12 b(t,X_t)^2 f_{xx}(t,X_t) \right)dt + b(t,X_t) f_x(t,X_t)\,dW_t } \]

Itô Calculus Rules

\[ (dt)^2 = 0, \quad dt\,dW_t = 0, \quad (dW_t)^2 = dt \]

Integral Form

\[ f(t,X_t) - f(0,X_0) = \int_0^t \left( f_s + a f_x + \frac12 b^2 f_{xx} \right)\,ds + \int_0^t b f_x\,dW_s \]

Example: \( f(x) = x^2 \)

Let \( X_t = W_t \):

\[ d(W_t^2) = 2W_t\,dW_t + dt \]

Multidimensional Version

For \( X_t \in \mathbb{R}^n \):

\[ df = \left( f_t + \sum a_i f_{x_i} + \frac12 \sum (\sigma\sigma^\top)_{ij} f_{x_i x_j} \right)dt + \sum \sigma_{ik} f_{x_i} dW_k \]

Generator Interpretation

Define:

\[ \mathcal L f = f_t + a f_x + \frac12 b^2 f_{xx} \]

Then:

\[ df = (\mathcal L f)\,dt + b f_x\,dW_t \]

Summary

\[ df = (f_t + a f_x + \frac12 b^2 f_{xx})dt + b f_x dW_t \]

The extra term arises from:

\[ (dW_t)^2 = dt \]

which reflects the quadratic variation of Brownian motion.
