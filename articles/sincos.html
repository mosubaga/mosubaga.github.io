Sine and Cosine in Sturm-Liouville Theory

The Sturm-Liouville Problem

Sturm-Liouville theory provides a general framework for a large class of second-order linear ordinary differential equations (ODEs). The standard form is:

$$-\frac{d}{dx}\!\left[p(x)\,\frac{dy}{dx}\right] + q(x)\,y = \lambda\,w(x)\,y, \quad a \le x \le b$$

Here $p(x)$, $q(x)$, and the weight function $w(x)$ are given real-valued functions on $[a, b]$, and $\lambda$ is the eigenvalue to be determined. Boundary conditions (Dirichlet, Neumann, or mixed) are imposed at $x = a$ and $x = b$.

Sine and Cosine as Eigenfunctions

The simplest and most instructive Sturm-Liouville problem is the one with $p(x) = 1$, $q(x) = 0$, $w(x) = 1$ on $[0, L]$:

$$-\frac{d^2 y}{dx^2} = \lambda\, y$$

With Dirichlet boundary conditions $y(0) = y(L) = 0$, the eigenvalues and eigenfunctions are:

$$\lambda_n = \left(\frac{n\pi}{L}\right)^{\!2}, \quad y_n(x) = \sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right), \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$$

With Neumann boundary conditions $y'(0) = y'(L) = 0$, the eigenfunctions are instead cosines:

$$\lambda_n = \left(\frac{n\pi}{L}\right)^{\!2}, \quad y_n(x) = \cos\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right), \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$$

With periodic boundary conditions $y(0) = y(L)$ and $y'(0) = y'(L)$, both families appear together, giving the full Fourier basis $\{1,\, \sin(2\pi n x / L),\, \cos(2\pi n x / L)\}$.

Orthogonality

A central result of Sturm-Liouville theory is that eigenfunctions belonging to different eigenvalues are orthogonal with respect to the weight $w(x)$:

$$\int_a^b y_m(x)\, y_n(x)\, w(x)\, dx = 0 \quad \text{for } m \ne n$$

For the sine eigenfunctions on $[0, L]$ with $w = 1$ this becomes the familiar result:

$$\int_0^L \sin\!\left(\frac{m\pi x}{L}\right)\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)dx = \frac{L}{2}\,\delta_{mn}$$

Orthogonality is what makes it possible to expand an arbitrary square-integrable function as a series of eigenfunctions — the basis of Fourier series.

Completeness and Function Expansion

Sturm-Liouville theory guarantees that the set of eigenfunctions is complete: any sufficiently well-behaved function $f(x)$ on $[a, b]$ can be written as

$$f(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} c_n\, y_n(x), \qquad c_n = \frac{\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\, y_n(x)\, w(x)\, dx} {\displaystyle\int_a^b [y_n(x)]^2\, w(x)\, dx}$$

For the sine basis this is exactly the Fourier sine series; for the cosine basis it is the Fourier cosine series. The Sturm-Liouville framework thus explains why these expansions work and extends them to a much wider family of special functions (Legendre polynomials, Bessel functions, Hermite functions, etc.) that arise from physically relevant choices of $p$, $q$, and $w$.

Connection to Partial Differential Equations

Sine and cosine eigenfunctions obtained from Sturm-Liouville problems appear naturally when solving PDEs by separation of variables. For example, the heat equation $u_t = \alpha\, u_{xx}$ on $[0, L]$ with $u(0,t)=u(L,t)=0$ separates into the Sturm-Liouville problem above in $x$, yielding the solution

$$u(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} c_n\, \sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) e^{-\alpha(n\pi/L)^2 t}$$

The coefficients $c_n$ are determined by the initial condition $u(x,0) = f(x)$ using the orthogonality integral above. The same logic applies to the wave equation, Laplace's equation, and Schrödinger's equation.
